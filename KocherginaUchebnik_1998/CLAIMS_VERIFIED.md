Her presentation principle is essentially the *paradigm-completeness* tradition of the Petersburg/Soviet school: give every form its rule and its exceptions, ordered by the grammatical system (present → future → aorist → participles), and trust the learner to drill the tables. That principle is well-suited to accuracy and to reading the classical language, and it explains the two genuinely good cases here: the aorist (HK-1) and the indefinite pronoun (HK-2) are presented at exactly the right altitude, with the diachronic and sandhi caveats a learner needs.

Where the principle fails is *frequency*. The tradition orders material by systematic regularity, not by how often a form is actually met — so a canonical "base rule" can be foregrounded over the statistically dominant realisation of it. The future stem (HK-4) is the clear case: -syá is taught as the single rule and -ṣya/-iṣya as consonant-stem "варианты", yet in the corpus the seṭ form -iṣya is the *majority* outcome (56.8% of distinct future forms). This is not a factual error — every surface fact is right — but a *pedagogical* one: the ordering hides the frequency distribution the learner will meet.
