## Abstract

Reference and pedagogical grammars of Sanskrit were written before machine-readable corpora existed,
and they teach the language through categorical rules — *"formed from all verbs by a single rule,"
"always feminine," "never accented."* Such phrasing was the only economical way to present a
paradigm, but it makes a falsifiable empirical claim about the attested language. We ask, for every
falsifiable assertion in one complete textbook — Kochergina's *Учебник санскрита* (1998) — whether
the Digital Corpus of Sanskrit (DCS-2021) confirms it. Of 43 verified assertions, 28 (65.1%) are
true against the corpus and the reference grammar (Whitney 1889); the textbook is factually accurate.
The contribution of this paper is the minority it is not: the 12 assertions (27.9%) whose fact-verdict
is *overstated* or *false* — the set of claims *the corpus does not confirm*. We give each such
divergence a class drawn from a four-way typology (over-generalisation, rule-real-but-marginal,
system-vs-usage, flat contradiction) and show that the divergence class aligns closely with the
pedagogical failure mode: over-generalisations read as *misleading*, frequency/conditioning
divergences read as *frequency-hidden*. The exemplar is the future stem, taught as a single rule in `-syá` while the
allomorph demoted to a "variant," the *seṭ* form `-iṣya`, is in fact the majority realisation (56.8%
of 2,618 attested future stems). The method — a reproducible two-axis register adjudicated against a
frequency corpus — and the typology generalise to any grammar whose ordering predates corpus
statistics; the per-grammar comparison across the reference grammars digitised in the same repository
is the natural extension.

## 1. Introduction

A reference grammar is a prescriptive-descriptive hybrid. It describes a language it also teaches,
and teaching demands compression: the learner is given a categorical rule ("the future is formed from
all verbs by adding `-syá`") rather than a distribution ("across the attested corpus, three
conditioned suffixes occur, in the following proportions…"). The categorical form is pedagogically
efficient and, for a language learned chiefly in order to *read* a fixed classical canon, largely
harmless. But a categorical rule is also a falsifiable empirical claim, and once the canon exists as a
lemmatised, morphologically tagged corpus, the claim can be checked.

This paper asks one narrow, falsifiable question of a single textbook: **for each categorical
assertion Kochergina (1998) makes, does the attested corpus confirm it, and if not, why not?** We are
not auditing the grammar for "errors." Most of its surface facts are correct — 65% of its verifiable
assertions are true against the Digital Corpus of Sanskrit and against Whitney's reference grammar,
and its failures are almost never errors of fact. What we measure is the gap between a rule *as stated
for teaching* and the *distribution a reader actually meets in the texts*, and we classify that gap.

The paper makes three contributions:

1. **A reproducible method** for corpus-adjudicating a grammar's categorical claims: a machine-readable
   register in which every falsifiable assertion carries a two-axis verdict — a *factual* verdict
   against a frequency corpus (with the number) and a *pedagogical* verdict on whether the presentation
   is defensible.
2. **A four-way typology of divergence** — the classes into which a "corpus does not confirm" claim can
   fall. The typology, not the list of failures, is the analytical object.
3. **An empirical finding**: on the full textbook, the divergence class predicts the pedagogical
   failure mode, and one class of the typology is systematically empty in a beginner's grammar — a
   result about *what kind* of thing a pedagogical grammar gets wrong, and how, that a flat error list
   cannot express.

We apply the method to the whole of Kochergina (1998), 43 verified assertions spanning all 40 lessons.
The study is single-book by design; §6 states this limitation plainly and §7 sketches the cross-grammar
extension.

## 2. Related work

**The corpus and its counts.** The empirical spine of this work is the Digital Corpus of Sanskrit
(DCS; Hellwig, 2010–, DCS-2021 release), the standard lemmatised, morphologically annotated corpus of
the language, and the neural segmentation and analysis pipeline built around it (Hellwig and Nehrdich
2018; ByT5-Sanskrit, Nehrdich et al. 2024). This literature *supplies* corpus frequencies and makes
them reliable; it does not adjudicate grammatical description against them. Our register consumes
DCS-2021 counts and does not re-derive them.

**Corpus-based evaluation of teaching materials.** Measuring a pedagogical text against a corpus is an
established programme in corpus and applied linguistics: since Nation's (2001) work on frequency-based
vocabulary selection, studies routinely compare a textbook's vocabulary or grammatical coverage against
corpus-frequency lists to judge what it foregrounds and what it omits (surveyed in a growing
systematic-review literature). We share that programme's method — corpus frequency as the yardstick for
a teaching artefact — but differ in *object*: that literature is overwhelmingly about *vocabulary* in
*L2 coursebooks*, whereas we adjudicate the *categorical grammatical rules* of a *reference/pedagogical
grammar*, and score each on a second, pedagogical axis rather than on a single coverage metric.

**Descriptive vs prescriptive grammar.** The conceptual move — a grammar's categorical rule against
what the corpus attests — is the corpus-linguistic distinction between *rules-as-regulations*
(prescriptive) and *rules-as-regularities* (descriptive). Corpus-driven reference grammar (Biber et al.
1999) repeatedly shows a canonical rule under- or over-stating attested usage. We inherit that framing,
but apply it not to induce a descriptive grammar *de novo* — rather to *audit a specific named grammar*
claim by claim against the corpus, with a reproducible register.

**Difficulty and sequencing.** Work on ordering pedagogical material by difficulty (Arase et al. 2022)
is methodologically adjacent — it too measures a teaching artefact against corpus evidence — but it is
trained on English and concerns *sequencing*, not the truth of categorical rules.

**Book-to-book relations.** Reconstructing relations between printed reference works (Hoenen 2015) is a
weak precedent for comparing grammars, and the mature stemmatic machinery for it lives in digital
humanities venues rather than in computational linguistics.

**What is new here.** Against those traditions the contribution is narrow and specific — *not* "compare
a textbook to a corpus," which is established. It is (a) the *target*: a reference grammar's categorical
*rules*, not a coursebook's word list; (b) the two-axis fact/pedagogy register and the four-way
divergence typology; and (c) the Sanskrit *seṭ/aniṭ* and ablaut targets, for which an ACL-Anthology
crosswalk finds no paper treating them as a corpus-adjudicated classification target, and none
adjudicating a Sanskrit grammar's categorical rule against corpus frequency. It is this last,
Sanskrit-specific gap — not the general idea of textbook-vs-corpus comparison — that the paper occupies.

## 3. Data and method

### 3.1 The claim register

The unit of analysis is the *falsifiable assertion*: a statement in the grammar about the language
that could in principle be checked against evidence ("the future is formed from all verbs," "stems in
`-ī` and `-ū` are always feminine"), as distinct from a definition, an exercise, or a paradigm table.
Every such assertion is one entry in a machine-readable register (`claims.yml`) carrying its location
in the text, the Russian original, a *falsifiable-as* operationalisation, its evidential sources, and
two verdicts:

- **`verdict_fact` ∈ {TRUE, OVERSTATED, FALSE, UNTESTABLE}** — is the assertion true against the corpus
  and the reference grammar, *with the actual number*.
- **`verdict_pedagogy` ∈ {JUSTIFIED, MISLEADING, RARE-AS-CENTRAL, FREQUENCY-HIDDEN, ORDER-QUESTIONABLE}**
  — independently of factual truth, is the *presentation* defensible: its ordering, its foregrounding,
  its hedging.

The two axes are deliberately separated because they come apart. A rule can be factually true yet
pedagogically misleading (a correct rule that hides how often its "exception" actually occurs), and the
separation is what lets the typology below distinguish a *factual* divergence from a *presentational*
one. This paper reads the fact axis: the "corpus does not confirm" set is exactly
`verdict_fact ∈ {OVERSTATED, FALSE}`.

### 3.2 Ground truth and the conflict rule

No single source is authoritative for every kind of claim, so verdicts are triangulated under a fixed
priority rule:

- **DCS-2021** (Hellwig, CC BY) is the authority for **frequency and attestation** — how often a form
  occurs, and whether it occurs at all.
- **Whitney (1889)** is the authority for **systemic grammatical fact** — what the language's paradigm
  licenses, cited by section.
- **Tolchelnikov-Talmud (2026)** — the repository's root-morphoclass dataset — is the authority for
  **root morphoclass**, i.e. which roots form which categories.

When the three disagree, the disagreement is **flagged in the entry, never silently resolved**. This
matters for exactly the claims this paper is about: several divergences are cases where the systemic
grammar licenses a form (Whitney) that the corpus under-populates (DCS), and collapsing that tension
would erase the finding.

### 3.3 The divergence typology

Each assertion in the "corpus does not confirm" set is assigned exactly one of four classes. The
typology is the paper's analytical contribution; the assignment (with a one-line rationale per claim)
lives in `divergence_classes.yml` and is joined to the register mechanically.

1. **Over-generalisation.** An absolute or near-absolute quantifier ("всегда / от всех / по единому
   правилу / никогда" — *always / from all / by a single rule / never*) where a well-defined,
   non-marginal exception class exists. The rule as taught is broader than the corpus and the reference
   grammar support.
2. **Rule-real-but-marginal.** A majority claim ("большинство / обычно" — *most / usually*) whose
   asserted share the corpus does not bear out: the pattern is real but is a minority, a plurality, or
   conditioned on a register or period the flat wording hides.
3. **System-vs-usage.** The reference grammar licenses a form the corpus barely uses — *licensed ≠
   attested*.
4. **Flat contradiction.** The grammar's stated conditioning — which allomorph or form appears, and
   when — does not match the corpus distribution; most sharply, a form demoted to a "variant" that is
   in fact the majority realisation.

### 3.4 Reproducibility

The pipeline is three hand-edited sources and two generators. `claims.yml` (the verified register) and
`divergence_classes.yml` (the class layer) are the sources; `verify_claims_dcs.py` re-derives every DCS
figure from the corpus tables, and `build_divergence_table.py` joins the register to the class layer
and emits the central table and its aggregation, refusing to run if any *overstated/false* claim is
left unclassified. Every number below is regenerable from these files (Python 3 + PyYAML; corpus
figures against the DCS-2021 release; Whitney 1889 cited by section).

## 4. Results

### 4.1 The textbook is accurate: a minority report

Of the 43 verified assertions — 43 register entries across 42 numbered claims, since the future stem
yields two (a universality claim, HK-4a, and an allomorphy claim, HK-4b) — the corpus confirms the
large majority (Table 1). The point of the paper is the minority it does not.

**Table 1. Register composition (Kochergina 1998, 43 verified assertions).**

| `verdict_fact` | n | share |
|---|--:|--:|
| TRUE | 28 | 65.1% |
| OVERSTATED | 11 | 25.6% |
| FALSE | 1 | 2.3% |
| UNTESTABLE | 3 | 7.0% |

Two-thirds of the assertions are simply true — the neuter nominative–accusative syncretism in all
numbers (Whitney §148), the one-final-consonant phonotactic constraint (§150), the instrumental of
agent in the passive (§275), the rarity of the conditional (269 tokens, 0.03% of 781,618 verbal
tokens) and of the middle precative (221 tokens, 0.03%) — all land exactly where Kochergina places
them. The textbook's factual accuracy is high; its failures are, almost without exception, failures of
*calibration*.

### 4.3 The flagship case: the future stem

Kochergina presents the future stem as formed *from all verbs by a single rule*, the accented suffix
`-syá`, with the consonant-stem outcomes `-ṣya` and `-iṣya` given as *variants* conditioned by
root-final phonology. The unifying "single rule" is the foregrounded fact; the split is a footnote.

Every surface fact here is correct — each allomorph exists, and the phonological environments
Kochergina names are real. But the corpus inverts the presentation. Across 2,618 distinct attested
future stems (18,004 tokens, 2.3% of 781,618 verbal tokens in DCS-2021):

| Allomorph | Distinct forms | Share |
|---|--:|--:|
| `-iṣya` (*seṭ*) | 1,487 | **56.8%** |
| `-sya` | 652 | 24.9% |
| `-ṣya` | 479 | 18.3% |

The form taught as the base rule, `-sya`, is a *minority* realisation (24.9%); the form demoted to a
consonant-stem "variant," `-iṣya`, is the *majority* (56.8%). The reason is that the real predictor is
not root-final phonology but the *seṭ/aniṭ* lexical class — whether a root takes the connecting vowel
`-i-` — a class that is widespread across the lexicon. This is why the future generates two entries in
our register: the universal framing "by a single rule" is an **over-generalisation** (class 1, HK-4a),
and the conditioning account, which implies `-iṣya` is a narrow special case, is a **flat
contradiction** of the distribution (class 4, HK-4b). A learner optimised on "the rule is `-sya`" will
mis-predict the single most common case. The *seṭ/aniṭ* distinction, whose corpus adjudication has no
ACL precedent (§2), is exactly what the categorical rule hides.

### 4.4 The one falsehood: the gender of ī/ū-stems

Only one assertion is *false* rather than merely overstated. Kochergina states that stems in `-ī` and
`-ū` are *always* feminine (HK-16). She means the derived *nadī-/vadhū-* feminine type, which is
indeed feminine; but the absolute "always" is contradicted by a standard, non-marginal set of masculine
`-ī/-ū` stems — *rathī* 'charioteer', *senānī* 'army-leader', *sudhī* 'wise', the monosyllabic *bhū-*
in compounds (Whitney §343–344, §352). A learner will mis-gender common words. It is the clearest
over-generalisation in the book, and the counter-class is concrete enough to make the failure a
teaching hazard rather than a technicality.

### 4.5 What the aggregation shows

**Table 3. Divergences by class, kind, and pedagogy verdict.**

| Divergence class | n | | Claim kind | n | | Pedagogy verdict | n |
|---|--:|---|---|--:|---|---|--:|
| 1 Over-generalisation | 8 | | universality | 7 | | MISLEADING | 8 |
| 2 Rule-real-but-marginal | 3 | | statistic | 3 | | FREQUENCY-HIDDEN | 4 |
| 3 System-vs-usage | 0 | | rule | 1 | | | |
| 4 Flat contradiction | 1 | | allomorphy | 1 | | | |

Three results follow.

**Failures cluster in the universal statements.** Eight of the twelve divergences are
over-generalisations, and seven of the twelve are grammatically *universality* claims — precisely the
statements that carry an absolute quantifier, where over-reach is riskiest. Kochergina's characteristic
error is not getting a fact wrong but stating a real tendency as a law.

**The divergence class predicts the pedagogy verdict.** The correspondence in Table 3 is exact, not
approximate: all 8 over-generalisations (class 1) carry the `MISLEADING` pedagogy verdict, and all 4
frequency/conditioning divergences (classes 2 and 4) carry `FREQUENCY-HIDDEN`. An absolute rule with an
unstated escape hatch reads as *misleading*; a minority or variant reframed as the norm reads as
*frequency-hidden*. The two axes of the register (fact and pedagogy) and the divergence typology are
not independent: the typology *explains* the pedagogical verdict. It is what a one-axis "list of errors"
cannot state.

We are cautious about the word *predict*. The pedagogy verdicts (H768) and the divergence classes
(this study) were assigned in separate passes under separate criteria, so the correspondence is not
tautological by construction; but both partly key on the same surface feature — whether the assertion
carries an absolute quantifier or a frequency word — so the alignment is better read as strong
convergence between two independent codings of the same claims than as a blind predictive test. On a
single book of twelve divergences it is a suggestive regularity, not a validated law; the cross-grammar
extension (§7), where the two codings can be applied to fresh material by different annotators, is what
would turn it into one.

**The empty class is itself a finding.** No "corpus does not confirm" claim falls into class 3,
system-vs-usage. That is not an accident of a small sample: the licensed-but-unused cases exist in
Kochergina, but they surface as *true* fact-verdicts flagged `FREQUENCY-HIDDEN` — the *aniṭ* set called
"a few roots" when it is a closed class of dozens (HK-26), the two-way palatal/`-h` reflex before the
future left unresolved (HK-21). *Licensed-but-unused* is, in a beginner's grammar, a presentational
problem inside an otherwise-correct rule, not a factual divergence — a distinction the two-axis design
surfaces and a single fact-axis would collapse. The class is reserved for the cross-grammar extension,
where a *system-sourced* grammar (Whitney, or a Pāṇinian derivation) is the object and licensing itself
is at stake.

## 5. Discussion

**A tradition, not a set of mistakes.** The pattern of divergence is coherent enough to name its
source. Kochergina's presentation follows the paradigm-completeness tradition of the Petersburg/Soviet
school: give every form its rule and its exceptions, order the material by the grammatical system, and
trust the learner to drill the tables. That principle is well-suited to accuracy and to reading the
classical language, and it produces the many clean true-and-justified anchors in the register. Its two
systematic failure modes are the direct cost of its virtues. **Universality overreach** (class 1) is
the completeness impulse stated too strongly: the beginner-facing rule takes an absolute quantifier
where a known exception class exists. **Frequency-blindness** (classes 2 and 4) is the system-first
ordering: material is arranged by systematic regularity rather than by how often a form is actually
met, so a canonical "base rule" is foregrounded over the statistically dominant realisation of it. The
typology is, in effect, a formalisation of these two impulses and their factual consequences.

**Why the two-axis design earns its keep.** A purely factual audit would report twelve failures and
stop. The pedagogy axis, joined to the divergence typology, turns that list into an explanation:
*which* categorical rules a system-first grammar will overstate (the universals), and *how* the
overstatement will read to a learner (as a hidden escape hatch, or as a hidden frequency). The exact
class⇄pedagogy correspondence (§4.5) is the evidence that the two are the same phenomenon seen from two
angles.

**Application: the reading site.** The register is not only an analysis; it drives a reading-site
overlay that badges each claim on the digitised textbook with its truth verdict and, where the number
is hidden, the corpus frequency. The design principle follows the finding: keep Kochergina's text
intact — it is accurate — and badge the two failure modes, the false/overstated universals with the
counter-class and the frequency-hidden claims with the corpus number, so the learner sees where an
absolute rule has an escape hatch and where a "variant" is in fact the norm.

## 6. Limitations

**Single-book.** The strongest limitation is scope: this is one textbook. Whether the same divergence
profile — universality-heavy, with an empty system-vs-usage class — characterises other grammars is not
shown here and must not be assumed. In particular, the attribution of the future-stem framing (§4.3) is
to Kochergina alone; whether Bühler, Knauer, or Zaliznyak present it identically is an empirical
question the cross-grammar extension answers, not a shared framing to be cited in advance.

**A single annotator.** Both codings the central finding rests on — the fact/pedagogy verdicts and the
divergence class — were produced by one author-and-pipeline, with no second coder and no reported
inter-annotator agreement. `verdict_pedagogy` and the divergence class are judgment calls, so the
class⇄pedagogy correspondence (§4.5) should be read as one annotator's coherent scheme, not as an
agreement-validated result. The remedy is a second independent coder on a sample of the register (and
the cross-grammar re-annotation, where fresh material is coded by different hands); until then the
finding is reported descriptively, and the numbers behind it — being mechanical counts over a public
register — are at least fully reproducible even where the underlying labels are subjective.

**The untestable bucket.** Three assertions are neither confirmed nor denied but *untestable* and are
excluded from the divergence set: the claim that the thematic aorist is the most frequent aorist type
(the DCS-2021 export does not tag aorists by formation type), and two assertions of precise percentages
— a 60/30/<10% semantic split of prefixed verbs and an *a*-privative share of prefixed nouns — whose
count base Kochergina does not disclose and which are therefore not reproducible. These are flagged, not
scored; conflating "the corpus cannot adjudicate this" with "the corpus denies this" would be a category
error.

**Corpus granularity.** DCS-2021 is a lemmatised relational export; some finer claims (per-type aorist
frequencies, a running-text phoneme census) exceed what its tags carry, and are flagged rather than
forced. A future re-run against a more finely tagged release could promote some untestable claims to
verdicts.

**Rights.** The source text of Kochergina (1998) is in copyright. This paper cites individual claims and
reports *aggregate* corpus numbers, both of which are defensible; a release of the underlying text as
data is not, and is out of scope.

## 7. Conclusion and future work

Measured against the attested corpus, Kochergina's Sanskrit textbook is factually accurate — two-thirds
of its verifiable assertions are simply true — and its failures are failures of calibration, not of
fact. Twelve assertions the corpus does not confirm fall into a four-way typology whose classes are not
inert labels: the divergence class predicts the pedagogical failure mode exactly, and the absence of
one class (system-vs-usage) is itself a claim about what a beginner's grammar gets wrong. The method is
a reproducible two-axis register adjudicated against a frequency corpus; the typology is portable.

The immediate extension is the per-grammar comparison. The register schema is multi-book from the
start, and the reference grammars digitised alongside Kochergina in the same repository — Bühler,
Knauer, Zaliznyak, Apte — can enter the same pipeline. The question that comparison answers, and that a
single book cannot, is *which grammar over-generalises most*, and whether the system-vs-usage class that
is empty for a beginner's textbook fills for a systematic reference grammar. That is where the typology's
reserved slot is tested, and where "grammar claims the corpus does not confirm" becomes a claim about
the genre rather than about one book.
