**6.**

So let me point out the main problem of the european attempts to describe sanskrit morphology.

**7.**

Let's take one simple rule from Whitney's grammar as the example. If A and B is pair of morphemes and A is a verbal root and B is the future suffix -sya-, A becomes guṇa - that is what Whitney says. And, as you can see, it works great...

**8.**

But sometimes it does not happen because some roots refuse to become guṇa and remain in vṛddhi or in basic grade. If we turn to Whitney\'s Roots we can find that among nearly eight hundred and twenty (820) independent verbal roots there are approximately one hundred and ten which do not have guṇa grade. Therefore, the efficiency of this simple rule is less than eighty-seven percent, and that is the problem that the Zalizniak-based approach is trying to solve - to decrease this tremendous number of exceptions.

**9.**

The first step of the approach is the division of sanskrit phonological processes into three groups.

**10.**

The first is pure alternation, which occurs in specific alternating phonemes and does not depend on the surrounding phonemes.

The second and the third processes are two different types of saṃdhi or the phoneme surroundings dependent transformation. The difference is that the second group of processes takes place only in the alternating morpheme of the verbal root while the third group, which involves the general rules of sandhi, occurs everywhere.

**11**.

The pure alternation consists of 13 alternation series which are 13 sets of basic, guṇa and vṛddhi grades of a specific alternating phoneme. The series A1, for example, has zero as the basic grade (as in 'santi\'), a-short as the guṇa-grade (as in 'asti\') and a-long as the vṛddhi-grade (as in the perfect form 'āsa\').

All other series number 1 differ from series number 2 only in the length of the basic grade (as √ji of I1 and √nī of I2). The only series that does not have a pair is the series L.

Here you can see all the reconstructed phonemes for basic grades such as ṝ-long in √pṝ 'to fill\', m-short in √gam, m-long in √śam 'to quiet\', n-short in √man and n-long in √jan 'to born\'. We use these reconstructed phonemes in cases where the basic grade of a series does not have one clear form due to verbal root sandhi. For example, √ji "to win" has a clear form basically as \"ji,\" while the root \"man\" does not; instead it has "ma", "man", and "mn".

**12.**

This is a comprehensive description of the rules of verbal-root sandhi, with the use of C for consonants, V for vowels, and E for an alternating phoneme of the verbal root. The only necessary requirement is that E belongs to the verbal root, while the other phonemes designated as C or V may belong to the root, suffix, infix or ending.

**13**.

These three groups of transformations take place in this sequence. For example, let\'s form a third plural perfect "they were born\" from the reduplicated root √jan of the N2 series. First, the alternation occurs when reconstructed n-long remains in the basic grade. Then the verbal root sandhi, where an alternating \"n\" preceded by a reduplicated syllable and followed by a vowel transforms into the consonant n. And third, the general sandhi, where \"n\" after j becomes ñ.

**14**.

One question remains: How do we choose the right grade during the alternation step? To answer it Zalizniak introduced the concepts of morphonological position and alternation type.

**15.**

Let\'s define morphological structure as a combination of two morphemes: a preceding morpheme A and a following morpheme B. In this structure, Zalizniak introduces the concept of morphological position. In our approach, it is defined as the expectation of a certain grade - basic, guṇa, or vṛddhi - in every A with a certain B.

For example of B let\'s take the ending -t of the third plural parasmaipada and some forms of the imperfect tense. The first is the root √as 'to be\' of the second-class stem, which directly attaches endings. So the root is A and the ending -t is B. Without sandhi and without the ī-long the final form is 'aast\' with guṇa 'a\' of the series A1.

Second is the fifth class stem āpnu- in which A is the suffix -nu- and B is the same ending -t. The final form is 'āpnot\' with the guṇa 'o\' of the series U1 in the suffix.

So, we could derive a rule that the ending -t expects guṇa-grade from every morpheme A whether it is a root, suffix, or infix in the case of the seventh present class.

**16**.

But it only expects guṇa from A and does not make A guṇa because not every morpheme has a guṇa-grade. For example, let\'s consider optative forms with the same ending -t.

In the first case, we have the suffix -yā- as A, and the ā-long is already in vṛddhi-grade. It does not take guṇa-grade, not only with the ending -t but never and the basic grade as well. The ending -t expects guṇa and this expectation mostly aligns with reality, but sometimes it does not. The same thing happens in the second example with the optative suffix -ī- as A that remains fixed in the basic grade.

Our goal is not only to establish rules for expectations, the rules for morphemes B, but also to establish rules for matching these expectations, the rules for morphemes A.

**17.**

Let\'s specify the morphological position in three expectations according to three grades that sanskrit has and that might me expected.

The first morphological position is the expectation of basic grade in A. For example, suffixes such as -ta of the past passive participle, -ya- of the passive stem and the third singular ending -te.

The second morphological position is the expectation of guṇa-grade in A. For example, the suffix of the future stem -sya- and the third singular ending -ti generally.

The third morphological position is the expectation of vṛddhi-grade in A. For example, the aorist suffix -s- and the ending -ti with open roots of the U1 series.

**18**.

This specification of morphological position is necessary to incorporate the concept of alternation type. Alternation type is defined as alternation pattern of the morpheme A in three morphological positions. In simple terms, which grade A takes in case of which expectation. There are 4 alternation types.

The first type is the most fair. A takes what is expected by B. In cases of the root √kṛ in the past passive participle, the suffix -ta- expects the basic grade and √kṛ remains in the basic grade; in the future stem, the suffix -sya- expects the guṇa-grade and √kṛ becomes 'kar\'. In the fourth aorist stem, the suffix -s- expects the vṛddhi-grade and √kṛ becomes 'kār\'. The morphemes of the first type have all three grades. The first type is the most numerous among all Sanskrit morphemes.

The second type works normally when guṇa or vṛddhi expected but on the expectation of basic grade it also gives guṇa as in the past passive participle of √svar 'to sound' when it does not become 'svṛta' or 'svūrta' bur remains in guṇa as 'svarita'. These morphemes only have guṇa and vṛddhi grades. The second type is approximately one and a half times less numerous than the first type.

The third type is the fixed basic grade. For example, √jṛmbh 'to gape\' does not have guṇa or vṛddhi grade. The third type is approximately five times less numerous than the first type.

The fourth type is the fixed vṛddhi-grade. For example, √dhau 'to run\'. There are nearly twenty morphemes of the fourth type in Sanskrit at all.

**19.**

And now we can take a look back at the European approach to formulating grammatical rules. When Whitney states that the future suffix -sya- causes the verbal root to have guṇa-grade, it generates 110 exceptions. However, if we rephrase this rule as follows: \"the future stem suffix -sya- expects guṇa from every morpheme A,\" its efficiency tends to reach 100% when we are aware of the alternation types of all the verbal roots.

So we complicated the rule. One task to make something guṇa and if it is going wrong - to do something by hand we replace by two tasks: first, identifying the alternation type of a morpheme A, and second, aligning the guṇa expectation with the pattern of the alternation type. And the number of exceptions is decreasing significantly.

**20**.

Now, I would like to talk about our goals and achievements.

**21**.

To describe Sanskrit alternation we should determine the alternation type to all morphemes that could potentially be an A. This includes verb roots, noun roots, suffixes, infixes, and prefixes. And we should also determine the morphological position in which every A is placed by every possible morpheme B. This includes suffixes, infixes, and endings.

**22**.

These goals primarily require a database of all Sanskrit morphemes with their morphological characteristics, such as alternation series, alternation type, morphological position, seṭ-quality, and present and aorist stem classes. The verbal root has all of them, and a database of nearly 820 verb roots with these characteristics has already been created.

**23**.

Our goals require the algorithmization of verb root reduplication in all forms where it occurs, and this task has been completed.

**24**.

Third, these goals require formulating the rules for the insertion of the connecting vowel -i- (also known as seṭ-quality). By solving this problem, we added 5 more subdivisions of veṭ. Pure veṭ is free to insert -i- in every applicable case, while other veṭ\'s have this freedom partly limited. The seṭ-quality is defined for every verbal root and verbal suffix. It remains to formulate the seṭ-quality rules for nominal derivation.

**25**.

Fourth, our goals require the creation of a tree encompassing all possible morpheme combinations. Currently, this has been accomplished for verbal derivation only. This tree describes 160 possible verbal forms. And for nominal derivation work has just been started. It takes another paper to properly discuss these trees.
