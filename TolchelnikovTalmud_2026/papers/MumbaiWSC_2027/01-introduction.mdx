# 1. Introduction

_Created: 12-07-2026 · Last updated: 12-07-2026_

The idea that Sanskrit lexicography needed a shared, corrigible digital home did not arise in a vacuum. At the 37th International Congress of Asian and North African Studies (ICANAS), held in Moscow in 2004 under the aegis of the Institute of Oriental Studies of the Russian Academy of Sciences, the author first encountered Peter Scharf's *Rāmopākhyāna* (2002), a Sanskrit reader with a detailed traditional classification of compounds, and — in conversation with visiting Japanese and Indian Indologists — learned that both kept extensive marginal corrections in their personal copies of Monier-Williams' dictionary. Private correction lists of this kind rarely survive their owners. The observation suggested a problem larger than aggregation: how can the perishable philological labour of individuals become recoverable, reviewable work that another person can continue?

The Cologne Digital Sanskrit Lexicon project (CDSL; also cited as the Cologne Digital Sanskrit Dictionaries, CDSD) is a long-term undertaking, begun in 1994, with one central aim: to be the authoritative home of validated digital Sanskrit dictionaries. Its distinguishing property among digital Sanskrit resources is that it is *maintained*: the source data are corrected continuously, and the corrections are versioned and documented (§5). Derivative websites that scrape Cologne data generally do not track these corrections, and most strip the structural markup — in particular the literary-source tags that link dictionary entries to the texts they cite — which this report argues is the collection's most valuable asset (§6).

The founding programme was stated by Kapp and Malten (1997) at the 10th World Sanskrit Conference in Bangalore:

> The Cologne Digital Sanskrit Lexicon (CDSL) project undertakes to digitize and merge the major bilingual Sanskrit dictionaries compiled in the 19th century. Its aim is to provide a basic lexical corpus to provide easy access to all available meanings of Sanskrit words and to allow the creation of a number of computer programs that will help to analyze Sanskrit texts. In the first stage Monier-William's Sanskrit-English dictionary (MW) has been digitized to be followed at a second stage by three other dictionaries (Cap, PW2 and Sch). All these have been structured and mostly unified to allow access to the meanings as developed by the different lexicographers. As a final goal it is hoped that a step can be taken towards an integrated Sanskrit word catalogue which codifies the distribution of lexical units in Sanskrit text corpora by linking them to the existing descriptions in dictionaries by a numeric system which functions as a placeholder for a word sense which can be expanded or changed.

(Kapp and Malten 1997, p. 1; quotation retained verbatim from the project PDF.)

The present report takes this 1997 statement as its yardstick but asks a causal question the programme did not pose: why did this particular digital dictionary collection remain corrigible after its founder retired and institutional stewardship changed? The proposed answer is **serial infrastructural conversion**. In 1994 institutional funding converted printed books into data; in 2004–2013 collaboration converted character-coded transcription into shared structure and procedures; in 2013 archival custody detached survival from its founder's active stewardship; from 2014 public versioning externalised decisions and corrections; and by 2026 stable files and identifiers made succession technically possible even though human capacity remained concentrated.

This explanation competes with three simpler ones. Exceptional individuals plainly mattered, but their labour became durable only where it left transferable artifacts. University hosting preserved servers, but it does not explain the public correction history or the collection's forkability. XML and Git supplied affordances, but technologies did not recruit, adjudicate, or maintain by themselves. The argument therefore concerns interactions among people, institutions, and technical form, not a technological march toward openness.

Section 2 surveys the dictionary collection and its genealogy. Section 3 reconstructs the institutional and technical transitions from 1994 to 2026. Section 4 describes the data architecture — entry identifiers, encoding, and headword model — as mechanisms of recoverability. Section 5 quantifies the correction process and its concentration. Section 6 examines the dictionary–corpus linking programme. Section 7 reports cross-dictionary measurements. Section 8 treats people and geography as networks of labour rather than metadata decoration. Section 9 states the limits of the surviving evidence; Section 10 identifies plans that follow from the audit; and Section 11 returns to the causal argument.

One correction of perspective, prompted by Peter Scharf's review of an earlier draft, should be made at the outset. CDSL did not develop in isolation. From 2004 to roughly 2013 the digitisation and markup programme was a coordinated effort between the University of Cologne (Thomas Malten), Brown University and later the Sanskrit Library (Peter Scharf, Malcolm D. Hyman), and Jim Funderburk, with funded projects whose reports and datasets survive; several of the design questions discussed in this report — whether to merge dictionaries, how to coordinate headwords, whether to adopt TEI — were deliberated in that period, and the conclusions reached then are stated here alongside the present-day evidence (§2.2, §4.4, §4.5). The Sanskrit Library's own reference works and specialised dictionaries, and Gérard Huet's Sanskrit–French *Héritage* dictionary, remain outside the CDSL collection but are part of the same ecosystem (§2.4).
