# 2. The Dictionary Collection

_Created: 12-07-2026 · Last updated: 12-07-2026_

## 2.1 The core and its genealogy

The initial plan of 1994 covered the most frequently cited Sanskrit–English dictionary — Monier-Williams (1899) — and three genealogically connected Sanskrit–German dictionaries: Cappeller's *Sanskrit-Wörterbuch* (1887), Böhtlingk's *Sanskrit-Wörterbuch in kürzerer Fassung* (1879), and Schmidt's *Nachträge zum Sanskrit-Wörterbuch* (1928). The German line forms a single tradition: the shorter Petersburg dictionary (PW) condenses the great Petersburg dictionary of Böhtlingk and Roth (PWG, 1855–1875); Cappeller abridges PW; Schmidt supplements PW; and Monier-Williams' dictionary was regarded by contemporaries — and by Böhtlingk himself, polemically — as substantially an English rendering of the Petersburg material (§7.2). The tradition has a modern continuation: the *Nachtragswörterbuch zu den Petersburger Wörterbüchern*, compiled at Halle in 2013–2016, is the first large addition to the Petersburg line since 1928.

Because the same dictionaries have been cited under different sigla for over a century, and because the project itself changed its identifiers as it grew, this report uses the current CDSL sigla throughout and provides a complete concordance in the list of abbreviations (Appendix A). Three renamings matter for readers of the older literature: the dictionary cited in early project documents as *Cap* is now **CCS** (Cappeller 1887); *PW2* is now **PW** (Böhtlingk 1879), reflecting the convention outside Cologne, where the shorter Petersburg dictionary has always been *pw* or *PWK*; and *Sch* is now **SCH** (Schmidt 1928). The variance is old: as documented in Gasūns (2006), the Petersburg dictionaries alone have circulated under at least five families of abbreviations (PW, Bö, PWG, BR, PW1; pw, KBR, PW2; PWN), depending on which feature — place, authors, size — a citing author treated as criterial.

Beyond the founding four, the collection grew in two directions. Historically backwards: Wilson (1832), the first Sanskrit–English dictionary, whose systematic etymologies remain unusual among its successors; Yates (1846); Goldstücker (1856, unfinished); Benfey (1866); the first edition of Monier-Williams (1872); and the Sanskrit–French and Sanskrit–Latin traditions (Burnouf 1866; Stchoupak 1932; Bopp 1847). Historically forwards: Apte's *Practical Sanskrit-English Dictionary* in both the 1890 edition and the revised three-volume edition of 1957–1959, and the *Encyclopaedic Dictionary of Sanskrit on Historical Principles* (Ghatage 1976–, in progress at Deccan College, Pune). Three English–Sanskrit dictionaries (Monier-Williams 1851; Borooah 1877; Apte 1920) were digitised in the funded collaboration with Peter Scharf's projects, although none had been part of the initial plan. Monolingual Sanskrit lexica — Vācaspatyam (1873), Śabdakalpadruma (1886), and the versified *koṣa*s of Halāyudha and Hemacandra — and onomastic and domain reference works (the Mahābhārata and Purāṇa indices, Edgerton's Buddhist Hybrid Sanskrit dictionary, Meulenbeld's plant names, epigraphical glossaries) complete the present collection of 44 works.

Of these, eight are treated as highest priority for correction and linking work: WIL (1832), MW (1899), AP (1957), PWG (1855), PW (1879), SCH (1928), VCP (1873), and SKD (1886) — the two great bilingual traditions in both languages, their supplements, and the two large monolingual lexica.

## 2.2 The merging question, closed

The 1997 programme spoke of digitising *and merging* the major dictionaries. The merge, in the strong sense, never happened, and the project's position today is that it should not. Two lines of evidence converge here.

First, the experimental merged displays built within CDSL itself — the combined Böhtlingk/Schmidt display, which merges PWG, PW, the *Nachträge*, and SCH with the then-known errors corrected — found no adoption. What users consult instead is the parallel display: the *dalglob* multi-dictionary view, which presents the results of many dictionaries side by side without merging them, and the `simple` search mode (2019), which retrieves one dictionary at a time and re-queries the same headword in another dictionary at one click.

Second, and earlier: the question had already been deliberated and decided in the funded phase. As Peter Scharf notes in his review of this report's earlier draft, the collaborators concluded that merging is counterproductive because a merged text destroys exactly the information a historical lexicographer needs — *who* added which meaning, and *when*; a combined interface was prototyped at the Sanskrit Library and ruled out in favour of look-up-once-then-requery, which is computationally cheaper and less confusing than concatenating many dictionaries on one page. The CDSL `simple` interface independently converged on the same design a decade later. This report treats the convergence as the settled answer: the unit of preservation is the individual dictionary; integration happens at the level of coordinated look-up and shared identifiers, not merged text (§4.4).

## 2.3 Meaning and verb-form tagging

The 1997 goal of "access to the meanings as developed by the different lexicographers" presupposes sense-level tagging. This exists, at the code level, only in MW, where semicolon groups separate senses, and even there the entry identifiers serve headwords, senses, and etymological segments alike. Cross-dictionary sense alignment — correlating Apte's senses with Monier-Williams' — has not been attempted, and the project does not currently regard it as a tractable priority; no reference lexicon of English (Webster, Collins) has been sense-aligned to another either, and the Sanskrit case adds a translation layer. Verb-form tagging is further along — substantial verb markup was added during the funded phase — but coverage is neither complete nor uniformly accurate, and the unstructured running text at the head of long verb entries resists tagging. A coordinated tag inventory for morphological identities has existed since 2013, agreed between the University of Hyderabad (Amba Kulkarni), the Sanskrit Heritage project (Gérard Huet), and the Sanskrit Library (Peter Scharf), and is published at sanskritlibrary.org/helpmorphids.html; aligning CDSL's verb markup with that inventory is the natural next step and is discussed in §10.

## 2.4 What is not in CDSL

The collection's boundaries should be stated as plainly as its contents. Gérard Huet's Sanskrit–French *Héritage* dictionary — born digital, morphologically complete, and under active development — is not part of CDSL. Neither are the specialised dictionaries and reference works of the Sanskrit Library (Scharf's *Rāmopākhyāna* glossary and others), nor the born-digital learner's dictionaries of the spoken-Sanskrit movement. The Digital Dictionaries of South Asia programme at Chicago (DDSA) hosts two Sanskrit dictionaries (Macdonell 1893/1929; Apte 1957); both are also in CDSL, where the print errors of the Chicago transcriptions have been progressively corrected. The Macdonell record is one work in two publisher states: the 1893 Longmans title is the original, while the 1929 Oxford University Press issue states that it reproduces that original photographically; it is therefore a reissue under a shortened title, not a revised dictionary. For Apte 1957, DDSA records an arrangement with Prasad Prakashan. This report treats that statement as an access-history fact, not as an independent jurisdiction-specific public-domain ruling; CDSL's own acquisition chronology likewise needs an archival locator before it can support a legal conclusion.
