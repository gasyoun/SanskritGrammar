# 6. Linking Dictionary and Corpus

_Created: 12-07-2026 · Last updated: 18-07-2026_

The final goal of the 1997 programme — an integrated word catalogue codifying "the distribution of lexical units in Sanskrit text corpora by linking them to the existing descriptions in dictionaries" — is the part of the vision that remains most active, and the part a reviewer of the earlier draft rightly asked to see developed in detail. This section states what the citation apparatus contains, what has been linked so far, by what method, and with what measured success and failure.

## 6.1 The citation apparatus

The Petersburg lexicographers were text editors as much as lexicographers: many of their sources existed only in manuscript, and over the 34-year span of PWG and PW (1855–1889) they repeatedly upgraded to better editions as these were printed — Kādambarī, Gobhilagṛhyasūtra, and Hāsyārṇava entered the dictionaries edition by edition, and for Bhartṛhari Böhtlingk compiled his own three-volume subhāṣita collection, twice. The consequence for linking is that a dictionary citation is a citation *of a particular edition*, often one no longer standard, sometimes one now rare; Jachertz's 1983 Cologne Magisterarbeit systematically documents editions underlying the PWG and PW abbreviations, and its scan and transcription now survive in the PWK repository. Several major works postdate the dictionaries entirely — the dramas of Bhāsa surfaced in 1910, the first chapter of Aśvaghoṣa's Buddhacarita was printed in 1892 — and the critical editions of the epics (Poona 1933–1966; Baroda) postdate them by a century, so the editions the dictionaries cite are never the editions modern corpora encode.

PWG cites a large and unevenly distributed body of literary sources across its seven volumes; the head of that distribution is dominated by the epics, the Ṛgveda, Pāṇini, and major narrative and legal works. Across the whole collection, canonicalising usable `<ls>` citation strings yields **828,505 citations resolving to 912 distinct texts** — the graph of which classical works each lexicographic tradition actually quotes, committed as the citation dataset in the `csl-atlas` repository. The citation density of the Vedic corpus alone separates the German tradition from the Anglo-Indian one measurably. Detailed source-by-source subtotals remain outside the article-specific frozen release and are therefore not reproduced here.

## 6.2 Link-target typology

Three kinds of targets receive links today:

1. **Semi-digitised page scans with a precompiled index.** For editions that exist only as extensive PDF scans (including the Bombay Mahābhārata of 1863 and Bombay Rāmāyaṇa of 1888), a volunteer builds a page index — a table assigning each PDF page its volume, page number, and textual span. A citation such as *MBh 13.1729* then resolves by lookup and arithmetic to a scan page, and large blocks of epic citations become clickable without full-text digitisation. Indexes of this kind have been contributed since 2020, largely by Anna Rybakova; the viewer applications live in the `sanskrit-lexicon-scans` organisation, with and without structural bookmarks depending on the edition.
2. **External digital corpora.** Where a maintained full-text resource exists, links target it directly: the Sanskrit–Russian parallel corpus for Vedic texts (Ṛgveda, Atharvaveda), aṣṭādhyāyī.com for Pāṇini sūtras, and — planned rather than deployed — the Digital Corpus of Sanskrit (DCS) for the GRETIL-derived texts it encodes.
3. **Internal targets.** Dictionaries citing dictionaries: the versified *koṣa*s digitised at CDSL are themselves link targets, with headword-level tagging complete for Amarakoṣa, Hemacandra's Abhidhānacintāmaṇi, and Halāyudha, while Medinīkoṣa still lacks manual tagging; and reference works such as Whitney's *Roots*, which MW cites natively.

## 6.3 Measured outcomes: one success, one negative result

The method was validated end to end on the Harivaṃśa: fitting a continuous-index model to the citation loci against the scan index produced a complete concordance between PWG's Harivaṃśa citations and scan pages, committed with its offset tables and shared-citation resolution data. The same programme for the Mahābhārata proper reached a full census and partial notes-apparatus verification, then was *suspended*: the Calcutta/Bombay vulgate text the dictionaries cite has no freely licensed electronic edition to fit against, and fitting against the critical edition would systematically mis-resolve, since the vulgate's Nīlakaṇṭha recension differs in exactly the passages a fitted index needs. The census and the abandonment are both committed; a free vulgate e-text would unblock the collection's largest single link block.

At the lemma level, the DCS–CDSL crosswalk links most of the corpus lemma inventory to CDSL headwords, while leaving a measured gap list. The `simple` search mode already consumes DCS frequency data; the debt runs in both directions, since DCS originally based its lemma list on MW. Against the frozen **323,425-headword** union, the dictionary–corpus concordance covers only a minority of entries. The distribution is Zipfian, as expected: the corpus attests the common vocabulary densely and the dictionaries' long tail of *samāsa*s and hapax entries barely at all. An integrated word catalogue is thus neither achieved nor refuted; its easiest, most frequent layer is the part built first.

## 6.4 Stable addresses

Linking runs in both directions, and inbound linking needs stable URLs. Entry-level URLs of the form `simple/mw/<headword>` — citable from Wikipedia, from digital editions, from other dictionaries — are the single interface feature with the highest expected return, and the standardisation path (the C-SALT APIs developed at Cologne's CCeH; RESTful access per dictionary) exists with working prototypes awaiting review. The `L` numbers (§4.1) make entry-level stability possible; what remains is exposing them as public, documented addresses rather than internal keys.
