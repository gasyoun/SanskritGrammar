# How the Cologne Digital Sanskrit Lexicon Endured (1994–2026): From Institutional Project to Shared Infrastructure

_Created: 12-07-2026 · Last updated: 12-07-2026_

**Mārcis Gasūns** — Sanskrit Zealots' Society, Obninsk, Russia — gasyoun@gmail.com

Prepared for the 20th World Sanskrit Conference (Mumbai, December 2027), Computational Sanskrit and Digital Humanities section.

## Abstract

The Cologne Digital Sanskrit Lexicon (CDSL), initiated at the University of Cologne in 1994, has outlived its founding department, successive technical regimes, and the retirement of its principal developer. This report asks how. It argues that CDSL endured through a sequence of conversions: privately held transcription became structured shared data; institutional custody became portable files; private correspondence became public version history; and dictionary entries became stable addresses against which corrections and links could accumulate. None of these conversions removed dependence on individuals, but each made their work more recoverable by successors.

The argument is tested against a versioned evidence snapshot rather than a single undifferentiated project total. In June 2026 the GitHub API inventory contained 78 repositories, while 76 were represented in the transformed activity tables; those tables contain more than 5,400 issue and pull-request records. The current collection inventory contains 44 dictionary and reference works. OBS-T records 52,498 documented correction events across 43 dictionaries and 208 release-safe corrector labels—not 208 adjudicated people—between 2014 and May 2026. Other committed measurements include a 323,425-headword union over the fifteen-dictionary comparison set, approximately 17.5 million markup-tag instances, and 828,505 canonicalised literary citations resolving to 912 texts. Correction implementation nevertheless remained concentrated: from 2019 through 2026 no year had more than five implementers, and the annual lead share ranged from 64 to 100 percent.

The result is neither a progress catalogue nor a heroic-founder narrative. CDSL's durable achievement is a corrigible scholarly object whose data can survive a change of personnel; its unresolved risk is that active maintenance still depends on a very small group. The report reconstructs that tension from the 1997 programme, committed data and code, attributed project records, and the public volunteer call of 27 June 2026. Email-dependent milestones awaiting archival review are explicitly excluded from load-bearing claims.

## Note on sources and provenance

This is a revised and re-grounded successor to the report reviewed for the 19th World Sanskrit Conference (Kathmandu, 2025). It incorporates the four anonymous reviews received there, the written response of Peter Scharf (who also supplied project reports and datasets from the funded phases of 2004–2013), and the recorded Cologne volunteer call of 27 June 2026 at which Jim Funderburk announced his retirement. Statements attributed to participants are identified as testimony rather than silently converted into repository facts. Quantitative claims are keyed to the frozen `a61-history-v1.0` evidence release; a data-availability statement and limitations section distinguish computed evidence, attributed archives/oral history, and interpretation.

A companion paper, *CDSL as methodological infrastructure: an empirical survey*, treats the measurement programme in full; the present report keeps the historical narrative in the foreground and uses the measurements as evidence.
