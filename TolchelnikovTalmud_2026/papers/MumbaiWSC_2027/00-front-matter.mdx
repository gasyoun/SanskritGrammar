# The Cologne Digital Sanskrit Lexicon Project (1994–2027): History, Infrastructure, and a Quantitative Audit

_Created: 12-07-2026 · Last updated: 12-07-2026_

**Mārcis Gasūns** — Sanskrit Zealots' Society, Obninsk, Russia — gasyoun@gmail.com

Prepared for the 20th World Sanskrit Conference (Mumbai, December 2027), Computational Sanskrit and Digital Humanities section.

## Abstract

The Cologne Digital Sanskrit Lexicon project (CDSL), initiated at the University of Cologne in 1994, is the reference digital repository of Sanskrit dictionaries. Over three decades it has evolved from a closed university initiative into a collaborative, openly versioned resource: since 2014 it has been maintained through the sanskrit-lexicon organisation on GitHub, which by mid-2026 comprised 76 repositories with more than 5,400 recorded issues and pull requests. The collection now offers 44 dictionaries — Sanskrit–English, Sanskrit–German, Sanskrit–French, Sanskrit–Latin, English–Sanskrit, and monolingual Sanskrit works — together with reference works such as Whitney's roots and a growing apparatus of cross-references between dictionaries, text corpora, and digitised page scans.

This report revisits the founding programme formulated by Kapp and Malten in 1997 and audits, point by point, what thirty years of work have and have not achieved. Unlike earlier accounts, the audit is grounded in measured, publicly committed datasets rather than participant recollection alone: a union index of 323,425 headwords across the collection; a correction ledger of 52,498 documented editorial events by 210 contributors over twelve years; a census of 17.5 million markup-tag instances across all dictionaries; a citation graph of 828,505 literary-source references resolving to 912 distinct texts; and cross-linkages to external morphology and corpus resources (185,803 entries aligned to the Sanskrit Heritage lexicon; 81.4% of the Digital Corpus of Sanskrit lemma inventory linked to CDSL headwords). The report also documents the project's institutional history — including the 2026 retirement of its long-time maintainer and the succession arrangements now in place — drawing on primary sources: the 1997 project report, private correspondence from 1997–2013, project reports by Peter Scharf from earlier funded phases, and the recorded volunteer call of 27 June 2026.

## Note on sources and provenance

This is a revised and re-grounded successor to the report reviewed for the 19th World Sanskrit Conference (Kathmandu, 2025). It incorporates the four anonymous reviews received there, the written response of Peter Scharf (who also supplied his project reports and datasets from the funded phases of 2004–2013), and the recorded Cologne volunteer call of 27 June 2026 at which Jim Funderburk announced his retirement. Statements attributed to project participants are cited to these sources. Quantitative claims are cited to committed datasets in the sanskrit-lexicon and related repositories; a data-availability statement appears at the end of the report.

A companion paper, *CDSL as methodological infrastructure: an empirical survey*, treats the measurement programme in full; the present report keeps the historical narrative in the foreground and uses the measurements as evidence.
