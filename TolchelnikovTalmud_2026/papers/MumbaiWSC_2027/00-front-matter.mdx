## Provisional conference abstract

_Working draft in the requested 250–300-word band. The official page located on 18 July 2026 states a submission deadline but no abstract word limit, so this label does not claim compliance with a venue limit._

The Cologne Digital Sanskrit Lexicon (CDSL), initiated at the University of Cologne in 1994, survived the retirement of its founder, changing technical systems, and the 2013 transfer of responsibility to the university's Data Center for the Humanities. This report asks what made that continuity possible. It argues that CDSL endured through serial infrastructural conversion: printed dictionaries became structured data; project-specific encodings became documented, portable formats; institutionally held files became preserved resources; private correction practice acquired public version history; and dictionary entries became stable targets for cumulative correction and linking.

The argument combines institutional records, published project documentation, attributed participant testimony, and a versioned evidence release. The June 2026 snapshot distinguishes 78 repositories in the GitHub API inventory from 76 represented in transformed activity tables. OBS-T records 52,498 documented correction events across 43 dictionaries and 208 release-safe corrector labels; these labels are not treated as 208 people. OBS-Q measures a narrower implementation layer: from 2019 through 2026, no year contains more than five correction implementers, and the annual lead share ranges from 64 to 100 percent. These populations show that broad correction intake and concentrated editorial authority coexist.

CDSL's durable achievement is therefore not scale alone but corrigibility: a scholarly object whose sources, identifiers, and procedures a successor can recover. Yet technical transferability has not eliminated dependence on a small maintenance group. By separating computed repository evidence from attributed historical testimony, and by leaving email-dependent milestones pending, the report presents CDSL as both a successful preservation infrastructure and an unresolved succession problem for long-lived digital humanities projects.

## Note on sources and provenance

This is a revised and re-grounded successor to the report reviewed for the 19th World Sanskrit Conference (Kathmandu, 2025). It incorporates the four anonymous reviews received there, the written response of Peter Scharf (who also supplied project reports and datasets from the funded phases of 2004–2013), and the recorded Cologne volunteer call of 27 June 2026 at which Jim Funderburk announced his retirement. Statements attributed to participants are identified as testimony rather than silently converted into repository facts. Quantitative claims are keyed to the frozen `a61-history-v1.1` evidence release; a data-availability statement and limitations section distinguish computed evidence, attributed archives/oral history, and interpretation.

A companion paper, *CDSL as methodological infrastructure: an empirical survey*, treats the measurement programme in full; the present report keeps the historical narrative in the foreground and uses the measurements as evidence.
