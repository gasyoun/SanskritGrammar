# 11. Conclusion

_Created: 18-07-2026 · Last updated: 18-07-2026_

CDSL did not endure because one institutional plan unfolded continuously from
1994 to the present. The strong merge envisaged in 1997 was abandoned; TEI was
tested but not adopted internally; corpus linking remains partial; and active
maintenance is still concentrated. Its history is instead a sequence of
contingent conversions, each prompted by a vulnerability exposed by the
preceding regime.

Institutional production made large-scale transcription possible but left
custody and interpretation concentrated. The 2004–2013 collaboration converted
character-coded text into shared structure and procedures. Transfer to the DCH
preserved the hosted collection after its founder retired and stewardship changed.
Public versioning from 2014 made a growing share of corrections and decisions
searchable and replayable. Stable identifiers, line-oriented sources, and
licensed repositories now allow another group to recover the data even if the
current service or team fails.

That mechanism also explains the limit. Infrastructure externalises memory; it
does not perform judgment. The 208 OBS-T corrector labels document a broad
intake record, while no more than five people per year implemented corrections
in the git era. CDSL has converted private scholarly labour into a durable
object more successfully than it has converted participation into distributed
editorial authority.

The project's historical significance therefore lies neither in size alone nor
in a claim to completion. CDSL is an experiment in making inherited
lexicography corrigible across changes of institution, technology, and
personnel. Its next transition will succeed if stable entry addresses,
documented adjudication, and explicit succession widen the group able to act on
the data — not merely the group able to download them.

_Dr. Mārcis Gasūns_
