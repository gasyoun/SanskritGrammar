# 3. History, 1994–2026

_Created: 12-07-2026 · Last updated: 19-07-2026_

The periods below are defined by changes in the custody and corrigibility of the data, not simply by software generations. They overlap: people and practices persist across each boundary, and no later regime wholly replaces its predecessor.

## 3.1 Institutional production (1994–2004)

The project was begun in 1994 by Thomas Malten at the Institute of Indology and Tamil Studies (IITS), University of Cologne. That date is recorded both in Kapp and Malten's 1997 project report and in the University of Cologne's 2016 institutional history. The founding method was manual data entry at scale: the original digital Monier-Williams was typed in Azhivaikkal (Thanjavur district, Tamil Nadu) by professional typists working from page images, using character codes that partially marked the divisions of the text. Malten's quality-control instrument was redundancy: for Burnouf's dictionary two typists entered the full text independently and the transcripts were compared — a method the project repeated a decade later when the Tirupati and Cologne transcriptions of Vācaspatyam were collated mechanically. Malten's interests were transcription fidelity and consistent transliteration principles rather than markup formalisms; the structural interpretation of his character codes came later and from elsewhere (§3.2).

An earlier attempt to digitise Indian lexical resources, at the University of Chicago in 1985, had included Monier-Williams and failed for lack of funding (Kapp and Malten 1997); the Cologne project is thus the second attempt at its own first stage, a point worth remembering when judging the pace of what followed. This first conversion — from printed books into an institutionally managed dataset — created the object later collaborators could preserve, but concentrated decisions, documentation, and access within one university project.

## 3.2 Collaborative formalisation (2004–2013)

Funderburk's attributed account dates the beginning of the collaboration to 2004. On the record from the volunteer call of 27 June 2026, he recalled that he had been interested in Peter Scharf's *Rāmopākhyāna* (2002), contributed corrections to it, and through it met Scharf and Malcolm D. Hyman. He had also tried, independently, to report obvious errors in Malten's digital Monier-Williams "and got nowhere." When the three met, "we thought there was a common interest and there was. And we chose up who did what" — Funderburk's chosen part being precisely the corrections to the Monier-Williams data that had gone unanswered (Cologne call, 27 June 2026, 02:50:04–02:50:32). Scharf then contacted Malten to propose collaboration. Funderburk further recalls approximately a year of correspondence among Funderburk, Malten, and Hyman before Malten's data model was fully understood. Public project records independently establish the institutional collaboration by 2006 and document its XML work and markup conventions in the May 2008 materials; they corroborate the collaboration without turning the unsupplied correspondence into documentary evidence.

Three durable technical decisions date from this phase:

1. **XML.** Malten's plain-text character codes were analysed, disambiguated, and transformed into XML by Scharf and Funderburk, with the assistance of Hyman and Chandrashekar. Corrections in this period were evaluated by Malten, Scharf, or Chandrashekar and implemented by Funderburk (Scharf and Funderburk, project documentation; cf. the MW header at the Sanskrit Heritage site).
2. **SLP1.** In 2008 the internal transliteration switched to the Sanskrit Library Phonetic encoding (SLP1; Scharf and Hyman 2009), whose one-character-per-phoneme design makes the round trip lossless — the Harvard-Kyoto convention used earlier could not represent a small class of Vedic forms with vowel hiatus without ambiguity.
3. **Scope.** The expansion from four dictionaries to the present collection was conceived around 2007, when Scharf's funded projects (NEH, and NEH-DFG jointly with Cologne) made systematic digitisation possible; most of the additions landed in 2010–2013. Reference works entered the collection in the same phase, notably Whitney's *Roots* with the assistance of Susan J. Moore, and the Mahābhārata Cultural Index (digitised 2013, NEH-DFG, jointly Cologne and Brown).

The first phase of Malten's own work was largely complete by 2004 and definitively by 2013, with occasional contributions to 2018. A University of Cologne presentation records that Malten retired in 2013 and the Data Center for the Humanities (DCH) became responsible for CDSL; the university's LAZARUS record independently dates the preservation project to December 2013–January 2015 and identifies CDSL as one of its three collections. This second conversion moved custody from a disciplinary project to preservation infrastructure. It explains survival of the hosted resource, though not by itself the later public correction regime. Felix Rau of the DCH remains the project's institutional link to the university.

## 3.3 Copyright, 2004–2025

The project's early posture on data release was cautious, and one episode from 2004–2005 illustrates the distance between the 1997 declaration — "it seems desirable to have high-quality Sanskrit databases freely available to all researchers" — and early practice. In 2004 Malten shared with the author a DVD of PWG and PW page scans, each file named for the first Sanskrit headword on the page. When the author then made the scans publicly available on his own server, Malten objected in writing. Malten's stated position was that while "the dictionaries as such are not copyright," the project's work on them — scanning, alignment, headword naming — was protected as a database under EU law, and that everything would become freely available for non-commercial use once the project was finished. This report records that as Malten's position in the correspondence, not as an independent legal conclusion. The scans were taken down at his request. The episode marks the contrast between restricted circulation in this attributed account and later public distribution; it does not establish a legal date on which the apparatus became open.

The present licensing status is no longer a matter of correspondence: as Peter Scharf notes, each dictionary's source file is accompanied by a TEI XML header declaring a Creative Commons licence (attribution, share-alike, non-commercial), so reuse conditions are stated in the data themselves.

## 3.4 Public versioning (2014–2026)

The sanskrit-lexicon organisation's public GitHub record dates from 2014, and a growing share of the project's coordination moved from private email to public issues and version history. This did not make the preceding history public, nor did it immediately produce pull-request review: the first pull requests visible in the bounded activity snapshot appear in 2019. It did, however, convert correction decisions from private holdings into records that could be searched, attributed, and replayed.

The June 2026 evidence snapshot contains two legitimate repository counts that earlier drafts conflated: 78 repositories in the GitHub API inventory and 76 represented in the transformed activity tables. The latter contain 5,413 issue-or-pull-request rows. `csl-orig` holds the canonical source files of every dictionary; `csl-corrections` holds the correction workflow and audit trail; presentation and API code live in `csl-*` repositories; and per-dictionary repositories host analytical discussions. Repository-level consolidation was itself a lesson learned: the initial one-repository-per-dictionary design produced duplication, and the sources were subsequently unified in `csl-orig` while the per-dictionary repositories were retained for research discussion.

Two further format events belong to this era. In 2016 Funderburk re-encoded PWG from its legacy transliteration into IAST-conformant data, an operation that surfaced diacritic-level inconsistencies still being corrected today (D. Patel, Cologne call, 27 June 2026, 01:08:32–01:09:02). In 2018 the sources moved to the current line-oriented format keyed by stable numeric entry identifiers (the `L` numbers; §4.1). One loss should also be recorded: Malcolm Hyman's original C-language transliteration code, which underpinned the early CGI interface, could no longer be rebuilt after his death in 2009, and — together with security reports against the ageing CGI — this is why substantial early material now sits in `legacy` directories, preserved but no longer served (J. Funderburk, Cologne call, 27 June 2026, 02:51:51–02:52:25).

## 3.5 Succession without deconcentration (2026)

On the volunteer call of 27 June 2026, Funderburk announced his retirement from the project he had, in effect, operated since 2004: "I guess this is sort of the time when I'm officially retiring... [I have] been unofficially retiring for the last two years or so... And we're in good hands." (Cologne call, 27 June 2026, 00:40:52–00:41:27). Daily correction handling had already passed to Dhaval Patel, who characterised his own capacity candidly — routine correction submissions continuously, larger structural work in "bouts of high productivity... in the middle of no productivity days" (Cologne call, 27 June 2026, 00:43:50–00:44:00). The succession model is thus Patel as maintainer of record for corrections and site operation, Funderburk as an occasional consultant, and the wider volunteer group (§8) carrying analytical and linking work.

Public version history, stable identifiers, and portable sources make this transition technically possible. They do not make it socially complete: OBS-Q shows no more than five correction implementers in any year from 2019 to 2026, with the annual lead share between 64 and 100 percent. The sequence therefore ends in a paradox central to this report: the data are more transferable than ever, while the labour required to maintain them remains highly concentrated.
