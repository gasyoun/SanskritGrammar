# 7. Cross-Dictionary Measurements

_Created: 12-07-2026 · Last updated: 19-07-2026_

Because all 44 dictionaries share one entry format and one transliteration, questions that lexicographic historiography could previously argue only from samples can now be computed over the whole collection. This section reports the measurements most relevant to the historical claims of §§2–3; the full programme, with methods and caveats, is in the companion paper.

## 7.1 The headword census

The union index across the collection contains **323,425 distinct normalized headwords**, each with per-dictionary provenance. Pairwise overlap across the fifteen largest dictionaries (105 dictionary pairs, Jaccard-scored) quantifies the genealogy of §2.1: the Petersburg-line dictionaries cluster tightly; Edgerton's Buddhist Hybrid Sanskrit dictionary is the extreme outlier with 58.7% of its headwords unique to itself — as its subject predicts — while Cappeller's abridgement contributes almost nothing unique (0.6%), as *its* genealogy predicts. The one measurement in this family that historiography most wants is the MW–PWG comparison, reported next.

## 7.2 Monier-Williams against the Petersburg dictionaries

Böhtlingk's accusation that Monier-Williams appropriated the Petersburg dictionaries' material — raised in his prefaces, amplified by Max Müller's involvement (Stache-Weiske 2015), and revisited by Zgusta (1988) on a selected case set — can now be examined against the full inventories. The locked comparison establishes that **94,753 lemmas are common to MW and PWG**. It also shows MW entries to be shorter on average and the dictionaries to share many citation sources, but the detailed length and citation-overlap subtotals are not retained because they are not in the article-specific evidence release.

What the numbers support is subtler than either polemic. MW is not a translation of PWG: a substantial part of its lemma inventory, dominated by *samāsa*s, is absent from the Petersburg dictionaries, and its entries are structured differently. But the citation-source overlap is consistent with a dictionary compiled, as Monier-Williams acknowledged and Böhtlingk protested, *with the Petersburg dictionaries open on the desk*. The remaining classical claim — Kapp and Malten's (1997, p. 6) footnote, "It may be surmised that MW has largely followed the order of meanings in PW1" (PW1 being their siglum for the large Petersburg dictionary, PWG here) — is testable by sense-sequence alignment over the common lemmas and has not yet been tested; it is registered as an open measurement.

## 7.3 Etymology across the traditions

Ten dictionaries carry extractable etymological or root-derivation data. Extracting them into a common root oracle and scoring cross-dictionary agreement shows the indigenous Pāṇinian tradition — the *koṣa*s, SKD, VCP — clustering closely on shared items, with Wilson (1832) as the principal outlier among the European dictionaries; part of Wilson's divergence is a measurement artefact of its affix notation, which the committed datasheet documents. For MW, the root inventory distinguishes entries the dictionary itself marks as genuine roots from the broader root-labelled set — the machine-readable descendant of the manual "genuine vs artificial" root analyses of the funded phase (§10.3). Exact totals are omitted because the current generated oracle no longer matches the stale figure in the earlier draft.

## 7.4 Interoperability measurements

Two alignment exercises measure how far CDSL's headword space can serve as a hub. A substantial MW–Sanskrit Heritage alignment provides a practical bridge between CDSL and computational morphology. The Whitney *Roots* crosswalk built in 2026 links root records across Whitney, MW, Apte, and DCS corpus attestations and is published as a FAIR dataset (CSV, RDF, SQLite with metadata). Both exercises expose the same two structural frictions identified in §4.4 — preverb-bearing headwords and stem-versus-nominative citation forms — as dominant sources of residual mismatch, which is independent confirmation, from alignment practice, of the analysis Scharf argued from lexicographic principle. The draft's former exact alignment totals were stale or insufficiently framed and are not retained.
