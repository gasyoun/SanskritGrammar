# 8. The People

_Created: 12-07-2026 · Last updated: 12-07-2026_

Thirty years of CDSL have been carried by remarkably few hands. The relevant populations must be kept separate: the table below is a curated historical roster, OBS-T has 208 release-safe submitter labels, the June Git snapshot has sixteen normalized non-bot identities, and OBS-Q finds at most five correction implementers in any year since 2019, with the annual lead accounting for 64–100% of correction commits. The last measure captures the project's principal operational risk: a wide correction intake rests on a very narrow implementation layer.

## 8.2 Jim Funderburk

Funderburk's path into the project is documented in his own words in §3.2: a mathematician-economist by background, he came to Sanskrit through Scharf's *Rāmopākhyāna*, and to CDSL through the errors in the digital Monier-Williams that he could not get corrected from outside. From 2004 to 2026 he was the person who turned every design decision into running code: the XML transformations, the SLP1 migration, the 2016 IAST re-encoding of PWG, the stable-identifier format, the display and download tooling, and the implementation of the correction stream — a body of work his collaborators on the 2026 call summarised less technically: "he has been the only workhorse who has been working" (D. Patel), "so many things at such a quality have been done in all these years" (M. Gasūns; Cologne call, 27 June 2026).

One design preference of his deserves a historical note because it shaped the architecture: the insistence, from the beginning, that the entire dictionary collection remain installable and usable offline. His stated image for it was being "in a cave in Tibet" with no Internet and still having one's Sanskrit dictionary (Cologne call, 27 June 2026). In practice very few people are known to run local installations, but the requirement kept the data model file-based, complete, and portable — which is also what made the collection forkable, measurable, and independent of any single server or institution. The archival value of that decision became obvious in 2013, when Malten retired and the DCH assumed responsibility for the data.

His retirement statement on the same call closes the era in the project's own register: "It's a big change for me psychologically to get out of that mode, but it's the right time to do it... We're in good hands."

## 8.3 The Hyderabad school

Nāgabhūṣaṇarāvu Kālepu, a retired electronics engineer from Hyderabad, developed the Telugu-and-Sanskrit dictionary portal andhrabharati.com from 2006, digitising and tagging some thirty dictionaries with his colleague Seshatalpa Sai before joining CDSL work in 2021 — where, by an irony the 1997 report could not have foreseen, he partially fulfilled Malten's founding wish of "connecting Sanskrit with Tamil vocabulary," from the Telugu side: on his estimate some 70% of Telugu lexis is Sanskrit-derived, and his portal's tagging makes the layering explicit. Within CDSL his method is systematic single-dictionary campaigns: after the headword-level cleaning of Vācaspatyam and Śabdakalpadruma in 2022, he began in 2025 an entry-level cleaning of Vācaspatyam against a new scan, a campaign expected to take well over a year. The recurrence of full cleaning rounds on a single dictionary is the realistic unit of work at this scale. The author-held email about the earlier Vācaspatyam re-entry remains `evidence_pending` and is not reconstructed here.

## 8.4 On geography

The geography changed with the organisation of labour: from a Cologne-centred institutional project (1994–2013) to a distributed GitHub collaboration whose sustained contributors have worked from Germany, the United States, Russia, India, and France. Repository locations do not explain motives by themselves. What can be documented are pathways: the Petersburg lexicographic tradition supplied objects and research questions for the Russian participants; Scharf and Funderburk connected Cologne to the United States and India; and Kālepu and Sai brought an existing Hyderabad dictionary-digitisation practice into CDSL. These networks, rather than national labels, explain how expertise travelled.

Pune, the home of two long-running historical dictionary projects—the *Encyclopaedic Dictionary of Sanskrit on Historical Principles* at Deccan College and the Prakrit dictionary at BORI—has not yet entered a comparable public-data partnership with CDSL. That difference should not be read as a judgment on pace. The *Thesaurus Linguae Latinae*, begun in 1894 and still incomplete, is a reminder that exhaustive historical lexicography is a century-scale undertaking. The relevant digital-history question is narrower: under what legal and institutional conditions can work produced over that timescale become publicly addressable and maintainable while compilation continues?
