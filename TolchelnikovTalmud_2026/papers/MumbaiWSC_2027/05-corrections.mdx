# 5. The Correction Process, Quantified

_Created: 12-07-2026 · Last updated: 12-07-2026_

The claim that distinguishes CDSL from its derivatives is not that its data are perfect but that they are *corrected*: errors have a reporting channel, a review, an implementation, and an audit trail. This section replaces the anecdotal version of that claim with the ledger.

## 5.1 Workflow

Corrections are never applied directly to the source files by hand. A reader reports an error — typically through the "Correction" link present on every entry page, which feeds the Sanskrit-Lexicon Correction Form — or through a GitHub issue. The report is evaluated (historically by Malten, Scharf, or Chandrashekar; in the GitHub era by Patel and the volunteer group), expressed as a *change file* recording the before/after state, and applied to `csl-orig` by script. The change files accumulate in `csl-corrections` as the audit trail. The server-side artefacts — displays, downloadable archives — regenerate from the corrected sources on the Cologne side.

## 5.2 Three kinds of change

Not everything that differs between the print original and the digital text is an error, and the project distinguishes three cases:

1. **Intentional print changes.** The scan reads X; the digitisation deliberately reads Y. For MW these are logged in `mw_printchange.txt`, begun in 2008; the file exists because such changes are invisible on the web display and would otherwise look like transcription errors. The largest single block of intentional changes is the incorporation of MW's own printed "Additions and Corrections" into the body of the dictionary. Corrections published in early reviews — Winternitz (1900) among them — are a recognised further source, only partially incorporated.
2. **Transcription errors.** The print reads X; the digitisation had Y; Y is restored to X. These are the "typos" left by the 1990s data entry, and the majority of correction traffic. Malten, working essentially alone, had no complete error-correction regime for typist output; some regions of the data, including Apte's citations, remain measurably error-prone.
3. **Conversion artefacts.** Differences introduced neither by the typists nor by intent but by the format migrations themselves — spacing normalised in one conversion and objected to in the next, elements dropped as redundant and later missed. These were historically the least documented category, which is precisely why the git-era ledger below matters: since the sources have been versioned, no class of change is undocumentable.

## 5.3 The ledger

Mining the correction-form record and the version history of `csl-orig` yields **52,498 documented editorial events over twelve years (2014–2026), across 43 dictionaries** (OBS-T release `a61-history-v1.1`). OBS-T contains **208 distinct release-safe corrector labels**. These labels are not equivalent to 208 adjudicated people: they include unresolved and pseudonymised form-era identities and occupy a different population from the sixteen non-bot Git identities in the June organisation snapshot.

The distinction changes the historical claim. OBS-T measures the breadth of the submission record; OBS-Q measures the much narrower set of people who implemented corrections in `csl-orig`. From 2019 through 2026 the latter never exceeded five in a year, and the leading implementer accounted for 64–100% of annual correction commits. Public participation therefore widened the intake channel much more than it widened editorial authority or maintenance capacity. The first individually submitted correction represented in the GitHub-era record came from Usha Sanka in 2014.

Issue-tracker volume tells the coordination story: more than 5,400 issues and pull requests across the organisation's 76 transformed-table repositories by mid-2026. One artefact in that series should be flagged so that it is not misread as a trend: the sharp rise in opened issues during 2025 was largely produced by a submission script that opened one issue per corrected line; the script now batches its reports into daily digests, and 2026 is on course to be the first year in which more issues were closed than opened (D. Patel, Cologne call, 27 June 2026). Patel's testimony also identifies persistent annual carry-over as the source of much of the open backlog; no exact annual estimate is used here.

Two honesty clauses belong next to these numbers. First, the pre-2014 correction history—the email era—is documented only where change files happen to survive; the TSV register of the correction form does not contain every change ever made, since batch structural corrections were sometimes applied without individual records. Second, exploratory capture–recapture work is usable only for the three dictionaries where the two correction eras overlap enough to estimate (PW, MW, and BUR); it suggests low completion but is not frozen here as an exact percentage and cannot be generalised collection-wide. The correction process is real and auditable within its recorded layers; it is neither exhaustive nor near completion.

## 5.4 What correction cannot fix

A correction pipeline repairs transcription; it does not repair interpretation. Entries where the print itself errs (a handful of Monier-Williams' readings are themselves considered errors of interpretation), places where dictionary structure was never marked up (Apte's untagged literary sources), and the growing category of *deliberately* non-standard forms that a naive spell-checker would "fix" all require philological decision, not process. The quality-assurance tooling has grown accordingly: a collection-wide n-gram membership oracle now flags suspect headwords for human review rather than auto-correcting them.
