# 5. The Correction Process, Quantified

_Created: 12-07-2026 · Last updated: 12-07-2026_

The claim that distinguishes CDSL from its derivatives is not that its data are perfect but that they are *corrected*: errors have a reporting channel, a review, an implementation, and an audit trail. This section replaces the anecdotal version of that claim with the ledger.

## 5.1 Workflow

Corrections are never applied directly to the source files by hand. A reader reports an error — typically through the "Correction" link present on every entry page, which feeds the Sanskrit-Lexicon Correction Form — or through a GitHub issue. The report is evaluated (historically by Malten, Scharf, or Chandrashekar; in the GitHub era by Patel and the volunteer group), expressed as a *change file* recording the before/after state, and applied to `csl-orig` by script. The change files accumulate in `csl-corrections` as the audit trail. The server-side artefacts — displays, downloadable archives — regenerate from the corrected sources on the Cologne side.

## 5.2 Three kinds of change

Not everything that differs between the print original and the digital text is an error, and the project distinguishes three cases:

1. **Intentional print changes.** The scan reads X; the digitisation deliberately reads Y. For MW these are logged in `mw_printchange.txt`, begun in 2008, with over 700 entries; the file exists because such changes are invisible on the web display and would otherwise look like transcription errors. The largest single block of intentional changes is the incorporation of MW's own "Additions and Corrections" (pp. 1308–1333, roughly 4,000 entries) into the body of the dictionary. Corrections published in early reviews — Winternitz (1900) among them — are a recognised further source, only partially incorporated.
2. **Transcription errors.** The print reads X; the digitisation had Y; Y is restored to X. These are the "typos" left by the 1990s data entry, and the majority of correction traffic. Malten, working essentially alone, had no complete error-correction regime for typist output; some regions of the data are error-prone in measurable ways — on the order of 5–10% of citations in Apte carry an error.
3. **Conversion artefacts.** Differences introduced neither by the typists nor by intent but by the format migrations themselves — spacing normalised in one conversion and objected to in the next, elements dropped as redundant and later missed. These were historically the least documented category, which is precisely why the git-era ledger below matters: since the sources have been versioned, no class of change is undocumentable.

## 5.3 The ledger

Mining the version history of `csl-orig` and the release archives yields a correction ledger of **52,498 documented editorial events over twelve years (2014–2026), across 43 dictionaries, attributable to 210 distinct contributors** (committed as `correction_events_*.csv` in the `csl-observatory` repository, with per-event edit-operation traces and latency data). A locus-resolved layer ties 39,540 of these events to their dictionary entry by `L` number; it shows, among other things, that 76% of locus-resolved corrections arrived in machine-assisted batches (concentrated in two dictionaries, BOR and LRV), while the long tail of human, one-at-a-time philological corrections is spread across the whole collection. The first individually submitted correction of the GitHub era came from Usha Sanka in 2014.

Issue-tracker volume tells the coordination story: more than 5,400 issues and pull requests across the organisation's 76 repositories by mid-2026. One artefact in that series should be flagged so that it is not misread as a trend: the sharp rise in opened issues during 2025 was largely produced by a submission script that opened one issue per corrected line; the script now batches its reports into daily digests, and 2026 is on course to be the first year in which more issues were closed than opened (D. Patel, Cologne call, 27 June 2026). Roughly seventy reported issues per year have historically remained unclosed, which over twelve years accounts for most of the open backlog.

Two honesty clauses belong next to these numbers. First, the pre-2014 correction history — the email era — is documented only where change files happen to survive; the TSV register of the correction form does not contain every change ever made, since batch structural corrections were sometimes applied without individual records. Second, scale: comparing the corrected error population against error-rate estimates from sampling suggests that twelve years of correction have addressed on the order of 10–14% of the errors present in the collection. The correction process is real, monotone, and audited; it is not near completion, and no honest report should imply otherwise.

## 5.4 What correction cannot fix

A correction pipeline repairs transcription; it does not repair interpretation. Entries where the print itself errs (a handful of Monier-Williams' readings are themselves considered errors of interpretation), places where dictionary structure was never marked up (Apte's untagged literary sources), and the growing category of *deliberately* non-standard forms that a naive spell-checker would "fix" (2,297 such forms across 33 dictionaries are now registered in a do-not-correct list) all require philological decision, not process. The quality-assurance tooling has grown accordingly — an n-gram membership oracle over the whole collection (6.6 million n-grams) now flags suspect headwords for human review rather than auto-correcting them.
