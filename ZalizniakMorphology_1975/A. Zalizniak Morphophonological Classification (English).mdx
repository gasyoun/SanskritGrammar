Translated from Russian by I. Tolchelnikov, edited by M. Ishimbaev.

In descriptions of the Sanskrit language (both ancient and modern), the concept of the root in general and the verbal root in particular is widely used. In this respect, Sanskrit language differs from most other Indo-European languages (especially modern ones) but shows similarities with, for example, the Semitic languages. This circumstance is evidently related to the fact that Sanskrit, like Semitic, is characterized by a high regularity in the morphological and morphophonological procedures used in deriving forms from a given root (due to which the root itself stands out very clearly[^1]).

At the directly observable ("surface") level, every root appears as a set of allomorphs. For example, the root meaning 'to cry' appears as *ru*, *ruv*, *ro*, *rav*, *rau*, and *rav*. Such a set is conventionally represented by a particular phonemic sequence (with an indication of its meaning), for example: the root *ru* 'to cry'. Usually this sequence is equal to one of the allomorphs, as in the example above (the so-called real method of designating a root), but not necessarily. For instance, the root with meaning 'to cut', represented by the allomorphs *kṛ*, *kir*, *kīr*, *kar*, and *kār*, is designated either *kṝ* (in the Indian grammatical tradition and in some European works) or *kṛ* (e.g., in Whitney's works \[7\], \[8\]); this is an example of the conventional method of designating a root. In both cases, it is assumed that the root designation allows to reconstruct all of its allomorphs. In other words, whether the designation is conventional or real, it can also be viewed as a "deep-level" morpheme that is transformed into the actually observable root allomorphs according to certain predetermined rules.

Thus, already in the works of ancient Indian grammarians (and subsequently in modern descriptions), the root primarily functions as a construct of synchronic description, ensuring the most concise formulation of morphological and morphophonological rules.

However, for practical purposes, merely having a list of allomorphs is not sufficient; it is also necessary to know the rules governing their distribution. Ideally, the root designation should allow one to determine, which allomorph of the root should appear in any given morphological formation[^2] (as well as presence of a connective vowel).

This requirement can be slightly loosened by considering only the most regular of those morphological formations that in principle can be derived from a verbal root. In the present paper, only the finite forms of the basic and so-called derived conjugations, participles, absolutives, and infinitives are examined --- that is what may be conventionally termed the verbal paradigm[^3].

However, the traditional system of root nomenclature does not achieve the aforementioned goal even with these constraints. This is evident from the fact that two roots with identical designations may exhibit different allomorphs in the same grammatical form. For example, cf. the *ta*-participle *uṣṭaḥ* (or *uṣitaḥ*) from *vas* 'to shine' with *vasitaḥ* from *vas* 'to clothe.' Evidently, to achieve the stated goal, additional information apart from the root designation must be employed. Such information could consist of assigning a root to a сertain class within a specially designed classification scheme (where, for instance, *vas* 'to shine' and *vas* 'to clothe' would belong to different classes).

The aim of the present paper is to construct such a classification of Sanskrit language verbal roots, where the root designation and its classificatory characteristics (i.e., the value of each classificatory feature for a given root) allow one to determine --- using standard general rules[^4] --- the correct allomorph of the root for any morphological formation within the verbal paradigm (as well as the presence or absence of a connective vowel).

The root designations adopted here are those used in Whitney's list \[8\][^5] (individual deviations are mentioned in the respective places). However, in the final section of this paper we discuss the possibility of designing an alternative designation system.

In some cases, the verbal paradigm cannot be derived from a single root surface form via general rules, making it necessary to include so-called root-variants (or variant roots). In such a case, a part of the paradigm is derived from one variant, while another part is derived from another. For example, the root 'to blow' has the variants *dham* and *dhmā*: the present tense *dhamati* is derived from the former, while the aorist *adhmāsīt* is derived from the latter. Many forms appear in two variants, such as the passive forms *dhamyate* and *dhmāyate*. To indicate such variability, the symbol // ('alongside with', 'in parallel with', reads as "*dham* along with *dhmā*") may be used. From the perspective of root classification, each root-variant constitutes an independent unit.

This paper also employs the concept of a quasi-root (termed secondary root \[7, §102\], quasi-root \[7, §66\], *fausse racine* \[4, §279\], etc.). A quasi-root is a stem (sometimes shortened) derived from a particular verbal form (most commonly the present tense), used to construct forms that are normally derived directly from the primary root. For instance, under the root *dā* 'to give', there is the quasi-root *dad* with the same meaning (extracted from present tense forms such as the 3rd plural *dad*-*ati*), from which, in particular, the *ta*-participle *dattaḥ*, the absolutive *dattvā*, and so on, are derived. From a synchronic point of view, a quasi-root is a special case of a root. Frequently, as in the example above, the quasi-root functions as a variant of the original root. A distinct subgroup consists of denominative quasi-roots --- roots postulated for denominative verbs, such as *mārg* 'to follow,' extracted from the present tense *mārgati* (which in reality is derived directly from a noun *mārgaḥ* 'track, path').

The present paper is based on the list of Sanskrit verbal roots documented in Whitney's list \[8\] excluding: a) roots for which no forms from the verbal paradigm are attested; b) roots marked by Whitney as "not a genuine root", "(possibly) artificial", "doubtful", or "possibly a misreading"[^6]; c) disyllabic roots (in Whitney's nomenclature)[^7]. Thus, approximately 750 out of 847 non-cross-referenced entries in Whitney's list were analyzed. Whitney's list \[8\] also served as the primary source for the verbal forms derived from each root. Additional references and corrections were drawn from \[1\], \[2\], \[3\], \[4\], and \[6\]. In most cases, only attested forms were considered; the places where records from ancient Indian grammarians were used are specially noted. Following Whitney's approach, the study encompasses linguistic material from the Vedic[^8], Epic, and Classical Sanskrit. For consistency, stress (except where specifically relevant) is not marked.

Three-Grade Alternation in Roots

The most significant alternation group in the morphophonological system of the Sanskrit language is structured as follows. There are three fundamental grades of alternation: 1) weak (or zero) grade; 2) guṇa (or full/middle/normal grade; 3) vṛddhi (or lengthened/strengthened grade)[^9]. The choice of the fundamental grade is morphologically determined and independent of the surrounding phonemes. However, within the same fundamental grade the alternating element can take various surface forms, and the choice between them does depend on the surrounding phonemes.

Traditionally, the term "grade" (zero, guṇa, vṛddhi) is applied not only to the alternating elements themselves (e.g., *i --- e --- ai*) but also to the root allomorphs containing them (e.g., *chid --- ched --- chaid*). Thus, expressions like "such-and-such grade of a root" are considered acceptable.

To prevent ambiguity in subsequent parts of the paper, comprehensive tables of the three-grade alternation are presented (Tables 1, 2; cf. \[4, §73\]). Each alternation series is denoted by a symbol (e.g., A~1~, U~2~, etc.), where the letter corresponds to the characteristic element of that series. Generalized symbols are also employed; for example, series U~1~ and U~2~ are collectively denoted as the "U-series"[^10]. Additional notations include: V = vowel; C = any consonant; T = obstruent; S = sonorant (i.e., *v*, *y*, *r*, *l*, *m*, or *n*[^11]); \# = word-form boundary[^12]. The notation +x means "in position before x", x+ --- "in position after x".

A remarkable feature of the three-grade alternation presented in these tables is its comprehensiveness: any allomorph of any (monosyllabic) verbal root of the Sanskrit language can, in general, be assigned to a certain cell in these tables --- i.e., its alternation series and grade can be identified. (However, this operation is generally not precise, as multiple cells may be applicable.) This allows to classify even the roots that show no actual grade alternation within a certain alternation series (but "frozen" at a particular grade)[^13].

NB: The three-grade alternations described below do not exhaust the non-positional alternations of vowels and sonorants. There are also other morphologically dependent transformations that are, as it were, superimposed on the described system. Let us note the most important of them: 1) the replacement of *a* (or zero) by *е* in the weak perfect stem that does not contain a reduplication, for example, *tepuḥ*, from *tap* 'to heat', *mene* from *man* 'to think'; 2) lengthening (*i* → *ī*, *u* → *ū*, as well as *ṛ* → *īr* or *ūr*) in the desiderative stem, for instance, *çuçrūṣati* from *çru* 'to hear'; 3) the opposite change in the 3rd class of aorist stem (for example, *adīdipat* from *dīp* 'to shine') and in some other cases. However, unlike three-grade alternations, these phenomena do not affect the classification of roots and are therefore not taken into account further.

Morphological Positions for the Roots

\[2\]

Let us call a morphological formation the following pair: a certain morphological structure + the corresponding grammatical or derivational meaning (or set of meanings). By morphological structure we will mean an indication of which affix (or affixes) is added to the root. The presence or absence of the connecting vowel i between the root and the suffix is ​​considered unimportant in this case[^20]. The alternation grade of the root is not considered to be a priori fixed for each morphological structure[^21] (since this is the exact issue we are trying to resolve). Examples of morphological formations: the 4th class of present tense (i.e., with the morphological structure "root + *ya*"); the so-called strong present tense 9 (i.e. the stem of the active forms of the singular, equal to the root + *nā*); infinitive ending in -*tum* \[i.e., with the morphological structure "root + *tum* (or *i-tum*)"\].

The choice of the required grade for a root in a certain morphological formation can be described according to the following scheme. There are three morphological positions in which the root can be: morphological position 1 --- the morphological position in which the zero grade of the root is expected; morphological position 2 --- the morphological position where the guṇa grade is expected; morphological position 3 --- the morphological position where the vṛddhi grade is expected. The morphological position depends, firstly, on the morphological formation, and secondly, on the phonemic structure of the root itself (in other words, the same morphological formation can constitute one morphological position for one root and another morphological position for another root[^22]). This dependence can be formulated in the form of general rules that allow one to establish the morphological position of an arbitrary root in an arbitrary morphological formation.

For most of the roots, the actual alternation grade always coincides with the expected one, but for other roots, there is no such coincidence or it does not always occur. Therefore, a "correct" type of roots and several "incorrect" types are distinguished. In the «correct» type, the choice of the alternation grade is thus determined directly by the general rules; in the "incorrect" types, in addition, appropriate amendments are necessary.

For the purposes of this work, there is no need to give the entire description constructed according to this scheme. As a detailed illustration of how the general rules provided by this scheme are constructed, the rules establishing the morphological position of an arbitrary root in the most important morphological formations of the verbal paradigm are given below.

Every root stands in morphological position 1 particularly in the following morphological formations[^23]: *ta*-participle (but not causative) (*kṛtaḥ*, *rucitaḥ*); *na*-participle (*chinnaḥ*); absolutive ending in -*tvā* (*chittvā*) and -*tya* (-*kṛtya*); passive stem (but not causative) (*chidyate*); weak perfect stem with reduplication[^24] (*cichide*, *cakre*, *jagme*); present tense 6 (*tirati*); both present tenses 5 (*kṛṇoti*, *kṛṇvanti*), 9 (*gṛbhṇāti*, *gṛbhṇanti*) and 7 (*chinatti*, *chindanti*); weak present tense of the 3rd class (*bibhyati*); Ātmanepada aorist stem 1 (*akṛta*).

Every root stands in morphological position 2, in particular, in the following morphological formations: infinitive ending in -*tum* (*kartum*, *rocitum*); stems of both future tenses (*vakṣyati*, *vaktā*, *bhaviṣyati*, *bhavitā*); stem of the 2nd person singular of the active perfect with reduplication (*cakartha*); present tense 1 (*bhavati*) and aorist stem 6 (*agāsiṣam*); strong present tense 3 (*bibheti*); stem of the singular of the active aorist 1[^25] (*asthāt*, *açrot*).

The following morphological formations constitute morphological position 3 for сertain types of roots, and morphological position 2 for others:

a\) stems of the 3rd person singular of the active perfect, of the 3rd person singular of passive aorist and of the causative. They constitute morphological position 3 for the roots ending in the *a*+C when in guṇa grade before a vowel[^26] (*tatāpa*, *atāpi*, *tāpayati*; *çuçrāva*, *açrāvi*, *çrāvayati*; *cakāra*, *akāri*, *kārayati*). For other roots they constitute morphological position 2 (*cicheda*, *achedi*, *chedayati*; *dadarça*, *adarçi*, *darçayati*);

b\) stem of the indicative of the active sigmatic aorist (its 4th and 5th classes). It constitutes morphological position 3 for roots ending in an alternating element of the series I, U or R (*açrauṣam*, *apāviṣam*, *akārṣam*, *atāriṣam*). For other roots it constitutes morphological position 3 in the absence of the connective vowel (aorist 4: *atāpsam*, *achaitsam*, *adrākṣam*, *ayāṁsam*) and morphological position 2 in the presence of the connective vowel (aorist 5: *agrabhīṣam*, *avediṣam*);

c\) stem of the singular active present tense 2. The roots ending in an alternating element of the U-series are here in morphological position 3 in the word-forms, where the root is followed by C (*stauti*). In all other cases, it constitutes morphological position 2 (*vetti*, *roditi*, as well as *astavam*, *bravīti*).

The following morphological formations constitute morphological position 2 for some types of roots, and morphological position 1 for others:

a\) aorist stem 2; it constitutes morphological position 2 for roots ending in an alternating element of the series A~2~, R, M or N (*akhyat*, *akarat*, *agamat*), and morphological position 1 for other roots (*achidat*, *avṛdhat*, *abhuvat*);

b\) aorist stem 3; it constitutes morphological position 2 for roots ending in an alternating element (*atuṣṭavat*, *acīkarat*, *ajīgamat*), as well as for roots with samprasāraṇa type of alternation YА~1~, VA~1~, RA~1~ (*ajigrabhat*), and morphological position 1 for other roots (*acichidat*, *adīdṛçat*);

c\) stem of the medial sigmatic aorist (its 4th and 5th classes); it constitutes the morphological position 2 for roots ending in an alternating element of the I, U, M or N series (*açroṣi*, *apaviṣi*, *agaṁsi*, *ajaniṣi*), as well as for roots with alternations YА~1~, VA~1~, RA~1~ (*agrabhīṣi*). For other roots, the stem of the medial sigmatic aorist constitutes morphological position 1 in the absence of a connective vowel (aorist 4: *achitsi*, *adṛkṣi*, *akṛṣi*, *asthiṣi*) and morphological position 2 in the presence of a connective vowel (aorist 5: *arociṣi*, *avardhiṣi*);

d\) stem of the desiderative with reduplication; it constitutes morphological position 2 for roots of the A, M or N series (*vivakṣati*, *tiṣṭhāsati*, *yiyaṁsati*, *jijaniṣate*), morphological position 1 for the roots of the I or U series ending in T (*cichitsati*, *rurudiṣati*). For other roots it constitutes morphological position 1 in the absence of a connective vowel (*didṛkṣate*, *titirṣati*, *bubhūṣati*) and morphological position 2 in the presence of a connective vowel (*ninartiṣati*);

e\) the stem of the present tense 2 (except for the singular active), plural and dual of the active aorist 1, of the present tense 4 and of the precative, as well as of absolutive ending in -*ya*. They constitute morphological position 2 for roots ending in an alternating element of the A~2~ series (*dānti*, *asthāma*, *gāyati*, *stheyāsam*[^27], -*sthāya*), and morphological position 1 for other roots (*rudanti*, *akran*, *nṛtyati*, *bhūyāsаm*, -*bhūyа*).

Roots with Full Alternation and with Partial Alternation

If the relatively few individual deviations (i.e., those that do not allow for the formulation of any general rules and can only be accounted for in a list of exceptions) are to be discounted, the following holds true for every root: in all morphological formations that constitute the same morphological position for a given root, the root appears in the same fundamental grade. Thus, if a particular root shows some deviation of its actual alternation grade from the expected one, this deviation normally encompasses an entire morphological position.

Further, the correlation across different roots between morphological positions and their alternation grades appearing in those positions is examined. It turns out that out of the 27 theoretically possible correlation types, only four are actually present (see Table 3)[^28].

The four types of roots thus will be distinguished. Roots containing *ā* (not before a sonorant) that does not alternate with *i* or *ī* --- such as *khyā* 'to see', *ās* 'to sit' --- can generally be classified as either type II or type IV; by convention, they will be assigned to type II.

It should be noted, however, that not every root is actually attested in the texts in all three morphological positions. Therefore, some roots with few attested forms cannot be definitively classified. Regarding morphological position 3, this position is entirely impossible for a significant portion of roots (in particular, type III consists exclusively of such roots). However, as shown in the table, in morphological position 3 (when it does occur) only one alternation grade appears: vṛddhi. Consequently, this position can be disregarded when determining a root type.

Roots of type I may also be termed roots with full alternation, while roots of the other three types may be called roots with partial alternation. The absence of alternation is thus considered as a special case of partial alternation.

The data shows that assignment of a root to one of these four types does not generally depend (at least in the majority of cases) on the phonemes surrounding the alternating element within the root. Cf. for example, the guṇa-grade allomorphs *sthā* (from the root √*sthā* 'to stand', type I) and *jñā* (from √*jñā* 'to know', Type II), or *smar* ('to remember', type I) and *kṣar* ('to flow', type II); the zero-grade allomorphs *mikṣ* ('to mix', type I) and *bhikṣ* ('to beg', type III); or the vṛddhi-grade allomorphs *dyaut* ('to shine', type I) and *ḍhauk* ('to approach', type IV). Even homonymy is possible here: cf. the guṇa-grade allomorphs *as* ('to be', type I) and *as* ('to throw', type II); *vas* ('to shine', type I) and *vas* ('to clothe', type II); *sev* ('to sew', type I) and *sev* ('to serve', type II) etc.

\[3\]

On the ground of the concepts of alternation series and root type, the convention for designating a root adopted by Whitney and in this paper can now be precisely formulated: in type II, the root designation is its guṇa grade; in type III, it is the zero grade; in type IV, it is the vṛddhi grade; in type I, for roots of alternation series A, M, or N, the designation is the guṇa grade; for other series, it is the zero grade. (Roots of series R~2~ have the peculiarity that they are designated as if they belonged to R~1~.) Thus, most of the allomorphs listed above also serve as the designations of their respective roots; however, 'to remember' is designated *smṛ*, 'to shine' --- *dyut*, and 'to sew' --- *sīv*. This shows that while the adopted designation system allows for distinguishing different types of roots in сertain pairs (e.g., *smṛ* vs. *kṣar*, *sīv* vs. *sev*), it does not achieve this goal in all cases (cf. *as* 'to be' and 'to throw', *sthā* and *jñā*, etc.).

Therefore, the rules for distributing roots between types can only rely on patterns of statistical nature and must necessarily include specific lists of roots. These rules are given below.

Type IV, excluding doubtful cases, consists only of the following roots: *ḍhauk* 'to approach', *mārg* 'to chase', *kāṅkṣ* 'to desire', *vāñch* 'to desire', *dhāv* 'to run', *dhāv* 'to rinse' (though, in the Ṛgveda, *dhūtaḥ* is still given according to type I; from the Samaveda onward, *dhautaḥ*), *cāy* 'to note, observe' (unless one includes in its paradigm the forms *cikyuḥ* and *citaḥ* from the semantically close root *ci*).

The distribution of the remaining roots between types I--III is systematically examined further by each alternation series.

Alternation Series A~1~

1\. General case. The norm here is type II (approximately 100 roots); examples (for morphological position 1): *gad* 'to say' --- *gaditaḥ*, *gadyate*, *jagade*; *as* 'to throw' --- *astaḥ*, *asyate*. In the post-Vedic period, only *as* 'to be' belongs to type I (present tense 2: *smaḥ*, *santi*, *syāt*; the vowel is preserved, but cf. imperative *edhi* and perfect *āsuḥ*). In Vedic, this also includes *ghas* 'to eat' (perfect *jakṣuḥ*, *jagivāṁs*-, aorist 1 *akṣan*, where *kṣ* &lt; *ghs*; -*gdha*- &lt; -*ghsta*-), *bhas* 'to devour' (3rd person plural present tense 3 *bapsati*, where *ps* &lt; *bhs*), *sac* 'to accompany' (3rd person plural present tense 3 *saçcati*, perfect *saçcuḥ*, *saçcivāṁs*-[^29]). Traces of Type I are preserved in Vedic for *pat* 'to fly, fall' (perfect *paptuḥ* and *petuḥ*, *paptivāṁs*-, aorist 3 *apaptat* and *apīpatat*). Later, *sac* ceased to be in use, while *ghas*, *bhas*, and *pat* gradually lost their old forms with the zero-grade root.

2\. Samprasāraṇa series. The roots of this group (about 60) are distributed between types I and II. Examples of type I roots (in morphological position 1): *yaj* 'to offer' --- *iṣṭaḥ*, *ijyate*, *īje* &lt; *i*-*ije*; *svap* 'to sleep' --- *suptaḥ*, *supyate*, *suṣupuḥ*; *grabh* (*grah*) 'to seize' --- *gṛbhītaḥ* (*gṛhītaḥ*), *gṛhyate*, *jagṛbhuḥ* (*jagṛhuḥ*). In addition to those mentioned, type I includes the following roots: Vedic[^30] *vyac* 'to extend', Vedic *myakṣ* 'to be situated', *vyadh* 'to pierce', *vac* 'to speak', *vad* 'to speak', *vas* 'to shine', *vas* 'to dwell', *vah* 'to carry', *vaç* 'to be eager', *vap* 'to strew', *vap* 'to shear', Vedic *vakṣ* 'to increase', *prach* 'to ask', *vraçc* 'to cut up', Vedic *krap* 'to lament' (cf. aorist 5 *akrapiṣṭa*; in Whitney's list, *kṛp*), as well as the Vedic *mrad* as variant to *mṛd* 'to crush', and the Vedic *bhraç* variant to *bhraṁç* 'to fall'[^31].

Deviations towards type II primarily affect the weak stem of the perfect: *paprachuḥ* (regular; already attested in the Brāhmaṇas); *vāvaçuḥ* (Ṛgveda), *vavakṣe* (Vedas), *suṣvape* (Upaniṣads, see \[4, §334\]), *vivyadhuḥ* (Epics; cf. Vedic *vividhuḥ*)[^32]; similarly *avivyacuḥ* (form of imperfect, Ṛgveda)[^33]. Other deviations: aorist 4 *apraṣṭa* (Classical), *vaditaḥ* (starting from Epics) and *vasitaḥ* (from *vas* 'to dwell', Classical), alongside the regular *uditaḥ* and *uṣitaḥ* (*uṣṭaḥ*); several other similar forms occur once or twice. The root *vyadh* in the post-Vedic period develops a parallel variant *vidh* (type I, alternation series I~1~, cf. Epic *veddhum*, *vetsyati*, *vedhayati*); ancient forms with the allomorph *vidh* (*viddhaḥ*, present tense 4 *vidhyati*) are reinterpreted as belonging to the root-variant *vidh* (rather than *vyadh*). The Vedic root *vakṣ* 'to increase' has from the beginning a variant *ukṣ* (type III; cf. *ukṣaya*-, aorist 5 *aukṣīḥ*, present tense 1 *úkṣant*, which, unlike *ukṣitaḥ* etc., cannot be derived from *vakṣ*).

Examples of Type II roots (in morphological position 1): *tyaj* 'to forsake' --- *tyaktaḥ* (*tyajitaḥ*), *tyajyate*, *tatyaje*; *vas* 'to clothe' --- *vasitaḥ*, *vavase*; *tras* 'to be terrified' --- *trastaḥ*, *tatrasuḥ* (and *tresuḥ*). This group also includes: *yat* 'to stretch', *yas* 'to be heated', *vyaj* (//*vīj*) 'to fan', *vyath* 'to waver', *kvath* 'to boil', Vedic *tvakṣ* 'to fashion', *vadh* (//badh) 'to slay', *çvas* 'to blow', *svaj* 'to embrace' (see also footnote 55), *svad* (//*svād*) 'to sweeten', *gras* 'to devour', *grath* (// *granth*) 'to tie', *prath* 'to spread', *vraj* 'to proceed', *hras* 'to shorten', and also roots with initial *ra*, for instance, *rakṣ* 'to protect', *ras* 'to roar'. Traces of type I are found in the Ṛgveda in *vas* 'to clothe' (*uṣānaḥ*, *uṣamānaḥ* --- once), *çvas* (*çuṣe*, *çuṣānaḥ*, etc.) and *svad* (infinitive -*sude*), in the Atharvaveda in *grath* (present tense *gṛṇatti*)[^34].

The root *çrath* 'to slacken' has varying forms (mainly Vedic; in the Classical language it receives the form *çlath*, type II): present tense 9 *çrathnāti*, *çaçrathe*, -*çrathya* according to type II[^35], but *çṛthitaḥ*, and present tense *çṛnthati* (once) according to type I. A number of rare roots are not attested in morphological position 1; such as, for example, *yabh* 'futuere', Vedic *vat* 'to apprehend', *trap* 'to be abashed'.

Alternation Series A~2~

1\. Roots ending in an alternating element (approximately 50). They are distributed roughly equally between types I and II. Examples of type I roots (in morphological position 1): *sthā* --- *sthitaḥ*, *sthīyate*, *tasthuḥ*, aorist 1 *asthita*, aorist 4 *asthiṣi*; *pā* 'to drink' --- *pītaḥ*, *pīyate*, *papuḥ*; *jyā* 'to overpower, injure' --- *jītaḥ*, *jīyate*; *hvā* 'to call' (//*hū*) --- *hūtaḥ*, *hūyate*, *juhuvuḥ*, aorist 2 *ahvat*. In addition to these, type I includes the following roots: *gā* 'to sing', *gā* 'to go', *dā* (//*dad*) 'to give', *dā* 'to divide, share', *dā* 'to bind', *dhā* 'to put', *dhā* 'to suck', *mā* 'to measure', Vedic *mā* (//*mi*) 'to bellow'[^36], *çā* 'to sharpen', *sā* (//*si*) 'to bind', *sphā* 'to fatten', *hā* 'to leave'; with samprasāraṇa alternation: *çyā* 'to coagulate', *vyā* 'to envelop', *çvā* (//*çū*) 'to swell', *vā* (//u*)* 'to weave'.

Minor deviations: -*dātaḥ* from *dā* 'to give' in the Ṛgveda (alongside *dattaḥ* and -*ttaḥ*); *hānaḥ* (Brāhmaṇas) and *hātaḥ* (Classical), *çātaḥ* (Epics), *çyānaḥ* (Classical) alongside the regular forms attested in the texts since the Vedic period: *hīṇaḥ*, *çitaḥ*, *çītaḥ* (and *çīnaḥ*); Vedic aorist 4 *ahāsi* (from *hā* 'to leave') and *pāsta* (from pā 'to drink').

The root *chā* 'to cut up' fluctuates between types I and II (*chitaḥ* and *chātaḥ*); the root *hā* 'to go forth' shows varying forms (present stem 3 *jihīte*, but *hānaḥ*, aorist 1 *ahāsta*).

The remaining roots ending in *ā* belong to type II; examples: *khyā* 'to see' --- *khyātaḥ*, *khyāyate*, *cakhyuḥ*, aorist 2 *akhyat*; *bhā* 'to shine' --- *bhātaḥ*; *vā* 'to blow' --- *vātaḥ* (*vānaḥ*). The root *rā* 'to give' (*rātaḥ*) shows faint traces of type I in the Vedas (present tense 3 *rirīhi*, *rarīdhvam* alongside *rarāsva*); a similar pattern is seen in the passive form *apsīyata* (Brāhmaṇas) from Vedic *psā* 'to devour' (*psātaḥ*)[^37].

2\. Roots ending in a consonant (approximately 30). They generally belong to type II; examples: *ās* 'to sit' --- *āsitaḥ*, *āsyate*, present tense 2 *āste*; *yāc* 'to ask' --- *yācitaḥ*, *yācyate*, *yayāce*. The sole exception is the root *çās* 'to order', which may be classified as a type I root with several individual peculiarities. Among forms constituting morphological position 1, some consistently show here the zero grade (aorist 2 *açiṣat*, active optative present tense 2 *çiṣyāt*), others show guṇa (perfect *çaçāsuḥ*, all forms of the middle present 2, e.g. *çāste*, *çāsīta*; in active present 2 --- 3rd person plural *çāsati* and imperative *çādhi*), while others fluctuate: regularly *çiṣṭaḥ*, but also *çāstaḥ* (from Sūtras onward) and *çāsitaḥ* (from Epics onward); -*çiṣya* (Brāhmaṇas, Upaniṣads) --- *çāsya* (from Epics onward); *çāsyate* (from Epics onward) --- *çiṣyate* (Classical). For the forms of plural and dual active present 2, ancient Indian grammarians prescribe *çiṣ* before consonants (*çiṣmaḥ* etc.) and *çās* before vowels; among attested forms, this rule is respected by *çiṣyāt* (from Upaniṣads onward) and *çāsati*, but contradicted by 2nd person plural *çāstana* in the Ṛgveda[^38].

Alternation Series I, U, R

1\. Roots ending in an alternating element. In this group, type I is the norm (about 120 roots); examples: *i* 'to go' --- *itaḥ*, *etum*; *nī* 'to lead' --- *nītaḥ*, *nínīyuḥ*, *netum* (*nayitum*), *nayati*; *çru* 'to hear' --- *çrutaḥ*, *çuçruvuḥ*, *çrotum*; *pū* 'to cleanse' --- *pūtaḥ*, *pupuvuḥ*, *pavitum*; *kṛ* 'to do' --- *kṛtaḥ*, *kriyate*, *cakruḥ*, *kartum*; *çṛ* 'to crush' --- *çīrṇaḥ*, *çīryate*, *çaçre*, *çariṣyate*.

Type II includes: a) *car* 'to move' (*caritaḥ*, *caritvā*, -*carya*, *caryate*, desiderative *cicariṣati*; but also *cīrṇaḥ* and *cīrtvā* following type I; *kṣar* 'to flow' (*kṣaritaḥ*), *tvar* 'to hasten' (*tvaritaḥ*), *sphar* (in Whitney's list, also *sphṛ*) 'to jerk' (*spharitvā*), Vedic *har* 'to be gratified' (present tense 4 *haryati*); b) quasi-roots ending in *ay*, derived from roots ending in *ā*, namely: *day* (from *dā* 'to share'), *chay* (from *chā* 'to cut up'), *vyay* (from *vyā* 'to envelop'), *hvay* (from *hvā*, *hū* 'to call'), *çvay* (from *çvā*, *çū* 'to swell'), *vay* (from *vā*, *u* 'to weave')[^39], as well as denominative quasi-roots *vyay* 'to expend' (from *vyayaḥ* 'expense') and *klav* 'to stammer' (from *viklavaḥ* 'confused')[^40]; examples: *dayitaḥ*, *chayitvā* (morphological position 1), *hvayiṣyati*, *çvayitum* (morphological position 2); c) the root 'to lie' traditionally designated *çī* (present tense 2 *çete*, *çayitaḥ*, perfect participle *çaçayānaḥ*, etc.; but perfect *çiçye* follows type I).

Varying forms occur in *av* 'to favor': -*ūtaḥ* (following type I), but -*avya*, precative *avyāt* (following type II). The following roots are not attested in morphological position 1: Vedic *dhav* 'to flow', *svar* 'to sound', Vedic *jar* 'to sing' (in Whitney's list, *jṛ*), *jhar* 'to fall', Vedic *phar* 'to scatter'. For roots *jvar* 'to be hot' and *tsar* 'to approach stealthily', the forms in morphological position 1 (*jvaryate* and -*tsarya*) are ambiguous.

2\. Roots ending in a consonant (the largest group of roots --- about 360). A small subgroup belongs to type II; examples (for morphological position 1): *sev* 'to serve' --- *sevitaḥ*, *sevyate*, *siṣeve*; *lok* 'to look' --- *luloke*; *garh* 'to chide' --- *garhitaḥ*, *garhyate*, *jagarhe*. Other type II roots: *edh* 'to thrive', *kṣvel* 'to play', *khel* 'to stagger', *ceṣṭ* 'to stir', *med* 'to be fat'[^41], *vell* 'to stagger', *heṣ* 'to whinny', *hreṣ* 'to neigh', *loc* 'to see, consider', *garj* 'to roar', *tarj* 'to threaten', *nard* 'to bellow', *arc* 'to shine, praise' (type II only from Epics onward, in Vedic *ṛc*), *spardh* 'to contend' (type II only from Epics onward, in Vedic *spṛdh*), *carv* 'to chew' (*carvyate*, *carvitaḥ*, but also *cīrṇaḥ* following type I). NB: guṇa in morphological position 1 for most of these roots is attested only in post-Vedic texts.

Some rarely encountered roots with *e*, *o*, *ar* are not attested in morphological position 1: *jeh* 'to loll, pant', *ned* 'to flow', *mreḍ* 'to repeat' (absent in Whitney's list), *mreḍ* 'to gratify', *mlech* 'to speak barbarously', Vedic *rej* 'to tremble', Vedic *ven* 'to long', *dol* 'to raise' (in Whitney's list, *dul*), *bol* 'to submerge' (in Whitney's list, *bul*), *kharj* 'to creak', Vedic *gard* 'to exult', *carc* 'to repeat', *tark* 'to think', *pard* 'pedere', *bharts* 'to revile', Vedic *bharv* 'to devour', Vedic *sarj* 'to creak'; also quasi-roots *bhreṣ* 'to totter' (see \[3, 11, 536\]), *yeṣ* (from *yas* 'to be heated'), *voc* (from *vac* 'to speak'), *mokṣ* (from *muc* 'to release'). Traditionally, these are designated and treated as type II roots. For variant-roots *oh*, *heḍ* (*hel*), *ej*, *eṣ* see below.

Root *arh* 'to deserve' is attested in morphological position 1 by only two Vedic examples: perfect *ānṛhuḥ* (following type I) and *arhire* (following type II).

Most other roots in series I, U, R are distributed between types I (approximately 200 roots) and type III (approximately 120 roots), generally adhering to this rule: roots in series I~1~, U~1~, R~1~ ending in a single obstruent are assigned to type I; others --- to type III. Type I examples: *ruc* 'to shine' --- *rucitaḥ*, *rocitum*, *rociṣyate*, *ruroca*, *rocayati*; *chid* 'to cut off' --- *chinnaḥ*, *chettum*; *vṛj* 'to twist' --- *vṛktaḥ*, *varjayati*. Type III examples (morphological position 2): 1) series I~1~, U~1~, R~1~: *bhikṣ* 'to beg' --- *bhikṣitum*, *bhikṣiṣyate*, *bhikṣayati*; *cumb* 'to kiss' --- *cumbitum*, *cucumba*; *jṛmbh* 'to gape' --- *jṛmbhayati*; quasi-root[^42] *gir* (from *gṛ* 'to swallow') --- *giritum*, *girayati*; 2) series I~2~, U~2~[^43]: *krīḍ* 'to play' --- *krīḍitum*, *krīḍiṣyati*, *cikrīḍa*, *krīḍayati*; *pūj* 'to reverence' --- *pūjayati*; quasi-root *pūr* (от *pṛ* 'to fill') --- *pūritum*, *pūrayati*.

As exceptions to the rule, the following roots are assigned to type I: a) *viṣṭ* 'to wrap' (*viṣṭitaḥ*, *veṣṭate*), *mikṣ* 'to mix' (*mimikṣe*, *mekṣayati*), *bhṛjj* 'to roast' (*bhṛṣṭaḥ*, *bharjjayati*), *mṛkṣ* 'to stroke' (*mimṛkṣuḥ*, *mrakṣanti*, *mrakṣaya*-; but also *mṛkṣaya*- following type III); b) *cur* 'to steal' (*acūcurat*, *corayati*), *lul* 'to be lively' (*lulitaḥ*, *lolati*), *mil* 'to combine' (*militaḥ*, *melayati*, but *miliṣyati* following type III)[^44]; c) *dīv* 'to play' (present tense 4 *dī́vyati*, *dyūtaḥ*[^45], *devitum*, *deviṣyati*, *dideva*, *devayati*) and similarly *dīv* 'to lament', *sīv* 'to sew', *ṣṭhīv* 'to spew', Vedic *srīv* (*çrīv*) 'to fail'[^46]; d) *rūṣ* 'to strew' (*rūṣitaḥ*, *roṣayati*). However, *ujh* 'to forsake' follows type III (i.e., *jh* behaves as two consonants).

A сertain group of roots with *ī*, *ū* (type III) presents its variants with *i*, *u* (type I) or with *e*, *o* (type II). Types I and III coexist for: *guh* 'to hide' (type I: *guhyate*, -*guhya*; cf. derivative *gohaḥ* 'den') // *gūh* (type III: present tense 1 *gūhati*, *gūhitum*, *gūhayati*, etc.); *uh* 'to remove' (-*uhya*, *oḍhum*) // *ūh* (present tense 1 *ū́hati*, *ūhitum*); *duṣ* 'to spoil' (*duṣṭaḥ*) // *dūṣ* (*dūṣayati*)[^47]; see also \[4, §63\]. Types II and III coexist for: *oh* 'to consider' (type II: 3rd person plural present tense 2 *ohate*) // *ūh* (type III: *ūhitum*, *ūhayati*). Other cases are ambiguous: Vedic *heḍ* 'to be hostile' (type II or I: only morphological position 2 is attested in *heḍaya*-, present tense 1 *heḍa*-) // *hīḍ* (type III: *jihīḍa*, etc.)[^48]; similarly *ej* 'to stir' (*ejati*, *ejayati*) // *īj* (in Ṛgveda; present tense 1 *īja*-); *eṣ* 'to move' (*eṣati*) // *īṣ* (*ī́ṣate*)[^49].

Special cases. The root *mṛj* 'to wipe' often shows vṛddhi instead of guṇa in morphological position 2: present tense 2 *mārṣṭi*, *mamārja*, *mārkṣyate*, *mārṣṭum* (*mārjitum*), *mārjayati* (but also *mrakṣyate*, *marṣṭum*, *marjayati*); it shows vṛddhi in morphological position 1 as well: *mārjitvā*, -*mārjya*, *mamārjuḥ*, alongside regular *mṛṣṭvā*, -*mṛjya*, *mamṛjuḥ*. According to ancient Indian grammarians, there is a group of rarely encountered roots of type I (so-called kuṭādi) which replace guṇa with zero grade in morphological position 2, except in perfect, causative, and passive aorist. Minor deviations from pure type I are not considered here.

Alternation Series L

The roots of this series (approximately 20) are assigned to type II; examples: *cal* 'to stir' --- *calitaḥ*, *calitum*, *cacāla*; *jalp* 'to murmur, speak' --- *jalpitaḥ*, *jalpyate*, *jajalpa*. The only exception is the root *kḷp* 'to be adapted', which is assigned to type I: *kḷptaḥ*, *cakḷpe* or *cākḷpe*, aorist 3 *acīkḷpat*, *kalpate*, *kalpayati*.

Alternation Series M and N

1\. Roots ending in an alternating element (approximately 50). These roots are distributed between types I and II. Examples of type I roots: *gam* 'to go' --- *gataḥ*, *gamyate*, *jagmuḥ*, *gantum*; *bhram* 'to wander' --- *bhrāntaḥ*, present tense 4 *bhrā́myati*, *bhramitum*; *man* 'to think' --- *mataḥ*, present class 4 *mа́nyate*, *mantum*; *jan* 'to give birth, be born' --- *jātaḥ*, present tense 4 *jā́yate*, *jajñe*, *janiṣyate*. Example of a type II root: *stan* 'to thunder' --- *stanitaḥ*, *stanitvā*, *stanati*.

Almost all roots of series M (approximately 20) are assigned to type I. Only *çam* 'to toil' and the variant *dham* of the root 'to blow' (//*dhmā*) unequivocally can be assigned to type II; additionally, *kṣam* 'to endure' variates between types I and II (*kṣāntaḥ* and *kṣamitaḥ*)[^50]. Among roots of series N (approximately 30), type II predominates numerically. The type I roots are listed below (this list includes the most frequently used roots of this series): Vedic *kan* 'to be pleased, enjoy', *kṣaṇ* 'to wound', *khan* 'to dig', *jan* 'to give birth, be born', *tan* 'to stretch', *dhvan* 'to cover'[^51], *man* 'to think', *san* 'to gain', *han* 'to smite'. The root *van* 'to win' is assigned to type I for Vedic language (-*vātaḥ*) but shifts to type II (*vanitaḥ*) from the Epics onward. Varying forms appear in Vedic *pan* 'to admire': *panitaḥ* (following type II) but *papane* (following type I).

2\. Roots ending in a consonant (approximately 60). The classification of this group presents certain difficulties, since some roots shift between alternation series M, N and series A~1~. Examples of type I roots: *stambh*[^52] 'to prop' --- *stabhitaḥ* (*stabdhaḥ*), *stabhyate*, Vedic *tastabhuḥ* (but in the Epics *tastambhe*), *stambhayati*; *dhvaṁs* 'to scatter' --- *dhvastaḥ*, *dhvasyate*, Vedic *dadhvase* (but in the Epics *dadhvaṁsire*), *dhvaṁsati*. Except these, the following roots predominantly not shifting to series A~1~ belong to this type: *añj* 'to anoint', *krand* (type I only in Vedic language) 'to cry out', *chand* 'to seem, please', Vedic *taṁs* 'to shake', *tañc* 'to coagulate', *baṁh* 'to make firm', *bandh* 'to bind', *bhañj* 'to break', Vedic *maṁh* 'to bestow'[^53], *raṁh* (type I only in Vedic language) 'to hasten', *randh* 'to be or make subject', *vañc* 'to move crookedly', *çaṁs* 'to praise', *çrambh* 'to trust', Vedic *çvañc* 'to spread', *skand* 'to leap', *skambh* 'to prop', *syand* 'to move on', *sraṁs* 'to fall'. A common feature of these roots is that the weak stem of the perfect with reduplication replaces the zero grade with guṇa starting from the Upaniṣads (see examples above). Since this replacement is systematic, it should be considered a historical shift in the norm: morphological position 1 was replaced by morphological position 2. Therefore, this does not affect their assignment to type I roots. Minor fluctuations towards type II mainly affect absolutives with suffixes -*tvā* and -*ya*, for instance, *syanttvā*//*syattvā*. Such fluctuations appear already in the Brāhmaṇas for roots like *syand*, *skand*, *sraṁs*, and later for *añj*, *bhañj*, *stambh*, *çaṁs*. Additionally: *çaṁsitaḥ* (Epic) alongside regular *çastaḥ*; *syandyate* (Classical). The roots *krand* and *raṁh* fully transition towards type II in the post-Vedic period: *kranditaḥ*, *raṁhitaḥ*.

A group of roots has two variants: a) a variant from series M or N (type I); b) a variant from series A~1~ (type II); for example: *daṁç* (//*daç*) 'to bite'. The distinction appears only in morphological positions 2 and 3 (cf. *daṁçayati* and *daçiṣyati*). In morphological position 1, both variants coincide: *daṣṭaḥ*, *daçyate*. Obviously, this is the primary cause of variation. Moreover, another factor facilitates variation (noted by Saussure \[5\]): in Vedic language, a present tense consisting of the root + thematic vowel *a* always has root stress if the root contains *a*. Thus, for *daṁç*, a present tense 6 \**daçáti* is impossible --- it automatically transforms into *dáçati*, which must be classified as present tense 1 based on its stress. Indeed, *dáçati* closely resembles typical present-tense forms of the 1st class like *pátati*, and by analogy with *pat*, the root *daç* is extracted (functioning as a variant of original *daṁç*). Notably, present-tense forms of the 1st class with zero root grade appear in nearly all roots shifting between M, N and A~1~: *dáçati*, *dábhati*, *rábhate*, *lábhate*, *nábhate*, *sájati*, *ácati*, etc. (therefore, below, when considering isolated roots, this fact is no longer noted).

List of roots belonging to this group[^54] is given here. Derivatives of *manth* 'to shake' and *math* 'to crush' are semantically distinct in the Vedas, see. \[3, II, 567, 578\], but later they blend, becoming mere variants. The root *mad* 'to be exhilarated, exhilarate' has a variant *mand* in the Vedas and the Brāhmaṇas (possibly a quasi-root extracted from the perfect, according to \[3, II, 580\]). The roots *daṁç* 'to bite' and *sañj* 'to hang' show A~1~ forms in the Epics --- *daçiṣyati*, *saktum*. The roots *granth* 'to tie' and *jambh* 'to chew up, crush' shift to A~1~ in the Classical period, i.e. replaced by variants *grath*, *jabh*. However, starting from the Epics, *raj* 'to color' shifts to series N, i.e. replaced by variant *rañj*. The roots *dabh* (//*dambh*) 'to harm', *rabh* (//*rambh*) 'to take hold', *labh* (//*lambh*) 'to take' predominantly present A~1~ variants; nasalization appears in the causative (*dambhayati* etc.), from the Epics onward, in the present tense 1 (*rambhate* alongside older *rábhate*), and rarely elsewhere (*dadambha*//*dadābha*; *arambhi*). The root *nabh* 'to burst' shows nasalization only in the causative (*nambhaya*-//*nabhaya*-); *aṁç* 'to attain' --- only in the perfect (*ānaṁça*); *majj* 'to sink' --- only in morphological position 2 before a consonant (except *y*): *maṅkṣyati* (cf. parallel *majjiṣyati*, also *majjitum*, *majjayati*, etc.)[^55].

The root *bhraṁç* 'to fall' (type I) has a Vedic variant *bhraç*, also type I (cf. -*bhṛṣṭaḥ*). The root *añc* 'to bend' is type I in Vedic language and has a variant *ac*, but in the post-Vedic period, only *añc* remains, fluctuating between types I and II (*acitaḥ* and *añcitaḥ*).

All other roots of series M and N ending in a consonant belong to type II, for example: *nand* 'to rejoice' --- *nanditaḥ*, *nandyate*, *nananduḥ*, *nandati*. Except this, the following roots are actually attested in morphological position 1: *aṇṭh* 'to visit', *kamp* 'to tremble', *lamb* (//*ramb*) 'to hang down', *vaṇṭ* 'to divide', *vand* 'to greet', *çaṅk* 'to doubt', *spand* 'to quiver', Vedic *dham* 'to run', Vedic *raṇv* 'to delight'.

Roots that underwent historical transitions from one type to another, as well as those showing fluctuations or varying forms, were counted twice in the calculations above. For type II, the first numeral indicates the number of roots definitively belonging to this type, while the second (following the + sign) denotes roots with an insufficient set of forms that could theoretically be classified as either type II or type I (according to tradition, assigned here to type II).

From the preceding analysis, it becomes evident that construction of a verbal paradigm requires not only the root designation but also an indication of whether it has full alternation (type I) or partial alternation (types II, III, or IV). This information cannot generally be deduced from the root designation. However, no further specification is necessary, as types II, III, and IV are easily distinguishable based on the grade in which the root is given. Thus, the most crucial classificatory feature of Sanskrit language verbal roots is what may be termed their degree of alternation --- their possession of either full or partial alternation.

Notably, the seemingly more intuitive division of roots into alternating (for example, *chid*, *yaj*, *tyaj*, *kṣar*) and non-alternating (for example, *garh*, *cumb*, *ḍhauk*) gives less suitable classificatory basis. Such division would, on one hand, fail to account for phonologically unmotivated distinction, for instance, between *yaj* and *tyaj*, while on the other hand, it would separate roots like *kṣar* and *garh*, whose differences are phonologically conditioned, into different classes.

Throughout the history of Sanskrit language, the class of fully alternating roots was decreasing, while the partially alternating class was continuously growing --- through the transition of the roots with full alternation into this class, as well as through the incorporation of various new roots (quasi-roots, products of phonological variation, loanwords, etc.). The primary starting point of this process was undoubtedly the replacement of the zero grade with the guṇa grade in the roots of series A~1~ without sonorants (such as *tap*, *bhaj*), where the regular zero grade proved phonetically problematic. The elimination of alternation also occurred early in roots of series A~2~ ending in a consonant. In both these root categories, type II was already dominating in the Vedic language. Later, the transition from type I to type II spread to series A~1~ roots containing sonorants and to roots ending in *ā*. In Vedic language, the guṇa grade in morphological position 1 is attested (in at least one form) for 22 roots ending in *ā*, while the zero grade appears in 24; an identical ratio is attested for roots of series A~1~ containing *ya*, *va*, *ra*. In the post-Vedic period, these ratios shifted in favor of type II: 27 --- 20 for roots ending in *ā*, and 29 --- 14 for roots with *ya*, *va*, *ra*. The described process reached roots of series I, U, R, L, M, N last: in the Vedas, the guṇa grade in morphological position 1 is attested for only 20 such roots (of which 14 belong to type II, while in 6 cases the relevant forms appear merely as sporadic deviations from type I)[^59]. Further, this number increased rapidly: for the Vedas together with the Brāhmaṇas it reached 33 (19+14 respectively), and for Sanskrit language as a whole --- 115 (75+40)[^60].

\[4\]

Traditional descriptions partially obscure the fundamental opposition between roots with full and with partial alternation: different manifestations of this opposition (for instance, *yaj* --- *tyaj*, *chid* --- *edh*, *man* --- *stan*) are treated without connection to each other and in substantially different terms. According to the traditional scheme (unlike in Tables 1 and 2), in series A~1~, M, N the zero grade coincides with the guṇa grade; thus, *taptaḥ*, *tyaktaḥ*, *stanitaḥ* represent the norm, while *iṣṭaḥ* and *mataḥ* constitute different types of deviations. On the other hand, roots like *chid* and *edh* are considered regular. Therefore, the relationships between allomorphs *ij* --- *yaj* and *chid* --- *ched* are not treated as analogous: *ched* is derived from *chid* (through guṇa operation), while *ij* is derived from *yaj* (through saṁprasāraṇa in the Indian sense).

Of course, the opposition between roots of different types could be formally represented as an opposition between different alternation series, for example: *i* --- *ya* --- *yā* (across three morphological positions: *iṣṭaḥ*, *yajati*, *iyāja*) and *ya* --- *ya* --- *yā* (or simply *a* --- *a* --- *ā*: *tyaktaḥ*, *tyajati*, *tatyāja*). If such a description were applied consistently, it would be equivalent to that proposed above and would give the same root types. The traditional system is inconsistent: alternations of the *a* --- *a* --- *ā* type are recognized for *tap*, *tyaj*, *stan*, etc., yet, for instance, the *e* in *edhitaḥ* is not considered a zero grade, nor is the *u* in *cumbitum* treated as a guṇa grade of any alternation series. As a result, the division of roots into types becomes considerably less apparent.

Roots of seṭ and aniṭ Groups

The specification of alternation degree accompanying a root designation enables the determination of all root allomorphs and their distribution, yet fails to ensure proper placement of the connective vowel. To achieve this goal, an additional classificatory feature must be employed --- namely, the opposition, well established already in the works of ancient Indian grammarians, between aniṭ and seṭ roots. The present paper cannot undertake a special consideration of this feature, limiting itself instead to certain aspects related to the «degree of alternation» parameter.

It is well-known that the appearance of the connective vowel in Sanskrit language shows multiple variation. The principal types may be distinguished as follows: a) aniṭ --- absence of connecting vowel (except in certain forms where nearly all roots display it, making it part of inflection); b) seṭ --- connective vowels appear wherever theoretically possible (specifically before suffixes beginning with a consonant excluding *y* and *n*, provided the root in the given word-form doesn't end in *ā* or an alternating element in zero grade); c) mixed types, divided into several subtypes; the most important of them are the so-called veṭ (optional connective vowel in every possible case) and a subtype where connecting vowels appear before *s* (*ṣ*) but not before *t*. It is more useful to classify roots with minor deviations from pure aniṭ or seṭ types within these categories (as presented in the calculations below)[^61].

Notes on the Morphophonological Root Nomenclature

\[1\]

Up to this point, we limited ourselves to a сertain method of root nomenclature that approximates the real method, though such limitation is not strictly necessary. Conventional designation systems generally permit the transfer (partial or complete) of classificatory information about an object into its very designation. Numerous examples of this practice exist in both linguistic and orthographic conventions --- cf. Russian "*вещь*" ('thing', feminine) and "*лещ*" ('bream', masculine), where the "*ь*" sign effectively functions as a classifier of the grammatical gender and does not affect pronunciation, or "*орёл*" ('eagle') and "*Орёл*" ('the city Oryol'), where capitalization distinguishes between homonyms; from linguistics, it is also worth mentioning Pāṇini, in whose nomenclature letters appended to a morpheme or a word-forms act as classifiers.

Regarding roots of the Sanskrit language, conventional nomenclature method already appear in works like \[6\] and \[4\], where a root "seṭ" parameter is marked by (i) in its designation (e.g., *rud*(*i*), *tar*(*i*)). This presupposes morphological segmentation as *rodi*-*tum*, *tari*-*tum* rather than *rod*-*i*-*tum*, *tar*-*i*-*tum* (cf. footnote 1). On the first glance, nomenclature like *tar*(*i*) appear substantially real, combining allomorphs *tar* (in *tarati*) and *tari* (in *taritum*). In reality, however, the (*i*) symbol also serves a purely conventional role, indicating that the zero grade here is *tīr* (*tīrṇaḥ*) rather than *tṛ*. Thus, instead of the somewhat ambiguous (*i*) symbol, the apparently conventional morphophonemic markers like *i2* or *H* (taken from Indo-European reconstruction) may be applied.

The following properties are ascribed to the morphophonemic symbol *H*: 1) *H* disappears before vowels; 2) between consonants (or between \# and consonant), *aH*, *iH*, *uH*, *ṛH*, *mH*, *nH* transforms respectively into *ā*, *ī*, *ū*, *īr* (*ūr*), *ām*, *ā*; 3) in other positions, *H* transforms into *i*. This allows us to represent numerous roots innovatively, e.g.: *rudH* 'to weep' (cf. *muc*), *puH* 'to purify' (cf. *çru*), *tṛH* 'to pass' (cf. *kṛ*), *janH* 'to give birth, be born' (cf. *man*). Similarly: *sthaH* 'to stand', *çaHs* 'to order', *jyaH* 'to overpower, injure', *hvaH* 'to call' (this notation brings roots of this series closer to A~1~ series roots like *pad*, *yaj*, *vac*). Fluctuations between "aniṭ" and "seṭ" parameters would appear as variant-roots, e.g.: *gup* (//*gupH*) 'to protect' (veṭ subtype).

An analogous approach can represent the "degree of alternation" parameter. For instance, we might conventionally mark vowel elements in roots with partial alternating with uppercase letters. The most significant graphic opposition would be: *a* (dropping in morphological position 1) --- *A* (not dropping in this position). Examples of root nomenclature combining both proposed conventional methods: *as* 'to be' --- *pAd* --- *gAdH*; *yaj*, *vac* --- *vadH* --- *tyAj* (//*tyAjH*) --- *vAsH* 'to clothe'; *kṛ* (or *kar*) --- *tṛH* (or *tarH*) --- *kṣArH*; *gam* --- *bhramH* --- *dhAmH* (//*dhmAH*, with element transposition); *man* --- *janH* --- *stAnH*; *sthaH* --- *bhAH*; *jyaH* --- *khyAH*. The details of such morphophonemic nomenclature are not considered here. The essential point remains clear: such nomenclature makes root classificatory characteristics redundant, as the root designation itself contains all the necessary information for verbal paradigm construction.
